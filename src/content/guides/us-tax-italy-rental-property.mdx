# US Tax on Italy Rental Property: 2026 Investor Guide

American investors who buy rental property in Milan, Tuscany, or the Amalfi Coast face two parallel tax systems. Italy taxes the rent where the asset sits. The United States taxes the same income because US citizens and permanent residents report worldwide income regardless of residency. The result is not double taxation at full rates in most cases, but it is also not automatic relief. You must file correctly on both sides, elect the right Italian regime, and track bank balances for FBAR and FATCA. This guide maps the full compliance stack for **us tax italy rental property** owners in 2026.

## Do US citizens have to report Italian rental income to the IRS?

Yes. US citizens, green card holders, and US tax residents must report gross rental income from Italian property on **Form 1040 Schedule E**, even if the property is managed entirely from abroad and even if no money is repatriated to a US bank account. The IRS taxes worldwide income. Rent collected in euros, paid into an Italian account, and spent locally still counts as US taxable income for the year it is earned.

Schedule E captures gross rents received, then allows deductions for ordinary expenses: property management fees, insurance, repairs, mortgage interest on an Italian loan, and foreign property taxes such as [IMU](/guides/imu-property-tax-italy/). Depreciation is also available on the building portion of cost basis using US rules, which often differ from Italian cadastral values. After net rental income is calculated, it flows to Form 1040 and is taxed at ordinary US federal rates up to 37%, plus state tax if applicable.

The critical planning point: Italian tax paid on the same rental income can offset US liability through the **Foreign Tax Credit (FTC)** on Form 1116, but the credit is limited to US tax attributable to foreign-source income. You cannot credit Italian tax against US tax on your salary or US dividends. A US-based CPA with cross-border experience should coordinate Schedule E, Form 1116, and any passive activity loss limitations if you actively participate in the rental activity from the United States.

## How does Italian tax on rental income work for US owners?

Italy taxes rental income where the property is located. Non-resident US owners are subject to Italian income tax on rent from Italian real estate. You have two main paths: progressive **IRPEF** (Imposta sul Reddito delle Persone Fisiche) on net rental income after allowable deductions, or optional **cedolare secca**, a flat tax on gross rent with no deductions.

Most foreign landlords elect cedolare secca because it caps exposure and avoids Italy's top IRPEF bracket of 43% plus regional surcharges. Under IRPEF, you can deduct a portion of maintenance, management fees, and interest, but high global income pushes the marginal rate up quickly. Under cedolare secca, the rate is fixed on gross rent: simpler administration, predictable cash flow, and usually better net results for affluent US investors who already pay significant US tax on other income.

Registration matters. Lease contracts must be registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate. Your Italian commercialista calculates and remits cedolare secca or IRPEF through the F24 payment system. Non-resident owners typically need an Italian **codice fiscale** and may appoint a fiscal representative depending on the rental model. Short-term tourist lets also require compliance with national CIN registration and local tourist taxes, as covered in our [Italy holiday let licensing](/guides/italy-holiday-let-licensing/) guide.

## What are the Italian vs US tax layers on rental property?

US owners operate inside two independent systems. Italy taxes the rental stream and annual property holding costs. The United States taxes net rental income after US deductions and credits Italian tax where allowed. The layers do not merge automatically.

| Tax Layer | Jurisdiction | What It Taxes | Typical US Owner Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Rental income tax | Italy | Gross or net rent from Italian leases | Cedolare secca 21% (standard LTR) or 26% (STR from 2nd unit) on gross; or IRPEF 23%–43% on net |
| Rental income tax | United States | Worldwide net rental income | Schedule E; ordinary rates; FTC on Form 1116 for Italian income tax paid |
| Annual property tax | Italy | IMU on non-primary residences | Paid locally; not deductible in Italy under cedolare secca; usually deductible on US Schedule E |
| Wealth / reporting | Italy | IVIE/IVAFE on foreign assets | Applies to Italian tax residents only; not relevant to typical US-resident owners |
| Bank reporting | United States | Foreign account balances | FBAR (FinCEN 114) if aggregate max over $10,000; Form 8938 if FATCA thresholds exceeded |
| Social security | Both | Employment/self-employment only | US-Italy totalization agreement does **not** apply to passive rental income |

Understanding where each obligation sits prevents the common mistake of paying Italian cedolare secca and assuming no US filing is required, or deducting IMU on the Italian return when cedolare secca blocks all expense deductions.

## What cedolare secca rates apply to US landlords in 2026?

Cedolare secca rates depend on contract type and how many short-term units you operate. The election is made per lease at registration. Once elected for a contract, you generally stay on cedolare secca for that lease term.

| Rental Model | Contract Type | Cedolare Secca Rate | Notes for US Owners |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Long-term residential | Standard free-market lease (4+4 years) | **21%** on gross rent | Most common choice for Milan/Rome apartments |
| Long-term residential | Subsidized concordato lease (3+2 years) | **10%** on gross rent | Only in designated high-demand municipalities with rent caps |
| Short-term tourist | Locazione breve, first STR property | **21%** on gross rent | Requires CIN; platform reporting to Agenzia delle Entrate |
| Short-term tourist | Second, third, fourth STR property | **26%** on gross rent | Each additional unit taxed at 26% |
| Short-term tourist | Five or more STR units | Commercial regime | Activity reclassified; VAT and corporate tax may apply |

Progressive IRPEF remains the default if you do not elect cedolare secca. Brackets start at 23% and rise to 43% above €50,000 of Italian-sourced income, plus regional add-ons. For a US investor with substantial US W-2 or portfolio income, IRPEF on Italian rent rarely beats a flat 21% or 26% on gross, especially when Italian deductions are limited for non-residents.

Model the choice against net yield, not gross yield. Our [gross vs net yield Italy](/guides/gross-vs-net-yield-italy/) guide shows how cedolare secca and IMU compress cash flow after all layers.

## Is IMU deductible when you elect cedolare secca?

No, on the Italian side. **IMU** (Imposta Municipale Unica) is Italy's annual municipal property tax on non-primary residences. Under cedolare secca, you cannot deduct IMU, condominium fees, management commissions, insurance, or maintenance against rental income. The flat rate applies to gross rent only. IMU remains a separate cash cost, typically 0.86% to 1.06% of cadastral value for second homes, paid in June and December installments.

On the **US side**, IMU is generally treated as a foreign property tax deductible on Schedule E, because it is an annual charge on real property imposed for the general public welfare. That US deduction reduces net rental income on your 1040 but does not reduce Italian cedolare secca due. You effectively get partial relief only through lower US taxable income, not through a dollar-for-dollar credit unless Italian income tax was also paid on the same base (which cedolare secca handles separately).

Investors who skip IMU in their [rental yield calculations](/guides/how-to-calculate-rental-yield-italy/) overstate returns. IMU is often €1,000 to €3,000 per year on a central Italian apartment and must sit in the expense stack even when invisible on the Italian income tax return.

## How does the Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116) work for Italian rental tax?

Form 1116 prevents double taxation by crediting foreign income tax paid against US tax on the same income category. Italian cedolare secca payments and IRPEF on rental income qualify as creditable foreign taxes for US purposes, subject to limitations.

The FTC is capped at the US tax attributable to foreign-source passive income in the rental basket. If US tax on your Italian net rent (after Schedule E deductions) is $4,000 and Italian cedolare secca is €4,200 (roughly $4,500 at 2026 exchange rates), you credit $4,000 and may carry excess credit forward up to ten years. If Italian tax is lower than US tax on the same income, you owe the difference to the IRS. State tax treatment varies: some states allow a FTC mirror; others do not.

Practical steps each year:

1. Convert gross rent and Italian tax paid to USD using IRS annual average exchange rates or spot rates consistent with your CPA's method.
2. Report gross rent and expenses on Schedule E; compute US net rental income.
3. Complete Form 1116 for the "passive category" income, listing Italian cedolare secca or IRPEF as foreign taxes paid.
4. Attach documentation: F24 receipts, commercialista annual statement, lease registration confirmation.

Depreciation taken on the US return can create timing mismatches: you may have low US taxable rent while still paying 21% Italian tax on gross, generating excess FTC carryforwards. That is normal in early ownership years. Coordinate with your CPA before electing cedolare secca if you plan aggressive US depreciation on a renovated Italian property.

## What US reporting forms do Italian property owners need?

Compliance extends beyond income tax. US owners with Italian bank accounts, escrow balances, or securities tied to the investment may trigger information returns even when no extra tax is due.

| Form | Agency | Trigger | Deadline (typical) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Schedule E** | IRS | Any Italian rental income | With Form 1040 (April 15; extension to October 15) |
| **Form 1116** | IRS | Claim FTC for Italian income tax paid | With Form 1040 |
| **FinCEN 114 (FBAR)** | FinCEN | Aggregate max balance of foreign accounts over **$10,000** any day in the year | April 15 (auto extension to October 15); file electronically only |
| **Form 8938 (FATCA)** | IRS | Specified foreign assets above threshold ($50k/$75k single US resident; higher if abroad or MFJ) | With Form 1040 |
| **Form 8621** | IRS | Ownership of PFIC (certain Italian funds/wrappers) | With Form 1040 |
| **Form 3520/3520-A** | IRS | Gifts from foreign entities or foreign trusts (if structure used) | Varies; penalties for non-filing are severe |

Italian rental operations commonly open a local checking account for rent collection and IMU payments. A single account holding €9,000 all year creates no FBAR obligation. Two accounts, a €6,000 operating account and a €5,000 IMU reserve, can exceed the $10,000 aggregate threshold on a strong euro day, triggering FBAR even if average balances look modest. Track **maximum** balances, not year-end snapshots.

Form 8938 includes not only bank accounts but also foreign stock, partnership interests, and certain debt instruments. Direct ownership of Italian real estate is reported on Schedule E; the deed itself is not a Form 8938 asset, but a foreign mortgage account might be. Thresholds differ for taxpayers living in the US versus those qualifying as bona fide residents of Italy under US rules.

## Does the US-Italy totalization agreement cover rental income?

No. The **US-Italy Social Security Totalization Agreement** coordinates social security taxes for work covered by each country's system. It prevents double contributions when an employee or self-employed person works across borders. **Passive rental income is not employment or self-employment income** under the agreement.

Owning and renting Italian property does not, by itself, create Italian INPS social security charges or US self-employment tax on rent for a typical passive landlord. If you operate five or more short-term units and Italy reclassifies the activity as a commercial enterprise, Italian social charges and VAT may apply locally, that is an Italian business classification issue, not a totalization treaty benefit. US self-employment tax could arise only if US law treats your activity as a US trade or business, which is uncommon for a passive overseas rental but worth confirming with counsel if you actively manage many units personally.

## What PFIC risks apply when US investors buy through Italian structures?

**PFIC** (Passive Foreign Investment Company) rules target US persons who own interests in foreign corporations or certain funds that earn predominantly passive income. **Direct personal ownership of Italian real estate is not a PFIC.** You hold a deed, not shares in a passive foreign company.

PFIC exposure appears when US investors buy through:

- Italian **SICAV** or retail funds marketed for property exposure
- Italian **SRL** or **SAPA** holding companies where you own stock and the entity's income is mostly rent or capital gains
- Certain **REIF** or pooled co-ownership vehicles structured as corporate entities

PFIC status triggers annual Form 8621 filing and default punitive taxation (excess distributions and deferred interest charge) unless you make a timely **QEF election** or mark-to-market election with a cooperative foreign fund. Compliance costs often exceed benefits for a single apartment. Most US buyers purchasing a home or flat for rent use direct ownership or, for liability planning, consult cross-border attorneys about Italian civil-law structures that do not create US PFIC issues.

If you are comparing the Article 24-bis **flat tax regime for new residents** who relocate tax residency to Italy, note that regime covers foreign-sourced income only. **Italian rental income remains fully taxable in Italy under standard rules** even if you pay €200,000 on your foreign portfolio. See our [Italy flat tax regime for new residents](/guides/italy-flat-tax-regime-new-residents/) guide for scope limits.

## Buyer Scenarios and Decision Framework

### Scenario A: US-Resident Passive Landlord (Long-Term Milan Lease)
A California-based investor buys a €420,000 apartment in Milan, leases it on a standard 4+4 contract at €1,600 per month, and uses a local manager at 10% of rent. Italian tax: 21% cedolare secca on €19,200 gross rent (€4,032). IMU: roughly €1,100. US tax: Schedule E reports gross rent minus management, insurance, IMU, and depreciation; Form 1116 credits Italian cedolare secca. FBAR: required if Italian operating account plus security deposit account exceeded $10,000 combined at any time. Best for: hands-off yield with predictable Italian tax.

### Scenario B: Dual-Property Short-Term Operator (Tuscany + Puglia)
A New York investor runs one STR in Florence (21% cedolare secca) and a second in Ostuni (26% cedolare secca). Higher Italian tax on the second unit, plus 25% management on gross bookings. US FTC still applies to both Italian flat taxes. CIN compliance and regional tourist taxes add local costs not deductible in Italy under cedolare secca. Five or more units would trigger commercial reclassification in Italy. Best for: yield-focused operators who accept compliance intensity; read our [Italy rental yield guide](/guides/italy-rental-yield-guide/) before scaling.

### Scenario C: Future Italian Tax Resident (Article 24-bis Candidate)
A Florida investor plans to relocate, pay the lump-sum foreign-income tax, and keep one Italian apartment rented while living in Rome. Article 24-bis does **not** exempt Italian rental income. Cedolare secca or IRPEF still applies to the Rome flat. US filing continues until you renounce US citizenship or otherwise end US tax residency, a rare step. FBAR/FATCA obligations continue for US citizens regardless of country of residence. Best for: wealthy relocators who model Italian local income separately from the €200,000 foreign-income lump sum.
