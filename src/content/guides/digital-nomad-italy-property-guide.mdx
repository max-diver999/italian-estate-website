## What changed in 2024 for remote workers in Italy?

Italy activated a dedicated Digital Nomad Visa pathway in 2024 under updated immigration flows (Decreto Flussi provisions for lavoro altamente qualificato and remote workers). The visa targets non-EU nationals performing remote work for employers or clients outside Italy. It sits apart from the [Elective Residence visa](/guides/italy-elective-residence-visa-property/), which requires passive income near €31,000 and prohibits all work.

Consulates now expect proof of remote employment or freelance contracts, six months of income history, a degree or three years of professional experience, and health insurance valid in Italy. Initial permits usually run one year, renewable through the Questura with continued employment evidence.

| Pathway | Income type | Work allowed | Best property strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Digital Nomad Visa | Remote salary or contracts | Foreign employer only | Rent furnished, buy after renewal |
| Elective Residence | Pensions, dividends | None | Buy or 12-month registered lease |
| EU citizenship | Any | Yes | Buy or rent freely |
| Schengen tourist | N/A | Remote work legally grey | Under 90 days only |

## 90/180 Schengen rule vs national visa: why it matters for property

Non-EU nomads without an Italian national visa may spend only 90 days within any rolling 180-day window across the Schengen Area. That cap makes it impossible to live year-round in Italy on a US, UK, or Australian passport alone, regardless of whether you own property.

Purchasing a flat does not change immigration status. An owner who overstays Schengen limits faces fines, entry bans, and future visa refusals. The fix is a Tipo D national visa (digital nomad or elective residence) followed by a permesso di soggiorno.

Practical sequence for nomads:

1. Test cities on Schengen stays under 90 days.
2. Apply for Digital Nomad Visa at the consulate with jurisdiction over your residence.
3. Enter Italy, file permesso within eight days.
4. Consider purchase after permesso renewal when your base city is proven.

## Milan and Rome: coworking, connectivity, and rent levels

Commute and airport access shape tenant pools and resale depth: properties within walkable services and rail links command premium rents versus car-only hill or coastal fringe stock. High-speed rail and motorway corridors often reprice peripheral tickets when corporate tenants accept 45-70 minute schedules instead of centro walkability premiums.

### Milan

Milan offers Italy's densest fiber footprint, late-night transport on core lines, and the largest English-speaking professional network. Coworking clusters include Navigli, Porta Nuova, Isola, and Porta Romana, with day passes from €20 to €35 and monthly desks from €250 to €450.

The [Milan property investment guide](/guides/milan-property-investment-guide/) tracks prime asking prices often €5,500 to €7,500 per m², with one-bedroom furnished rentals at €1,400 to €2,200 per month in well-connected zones. Nomads who buy should verify condominium rules on short lets and home office use.

## Buying vs long-term rent: a nomad decision framework

Match budget, hold period, and income target to the district cluster that actually delivers those outcomes — generic centro advice often overpays for liquidity while ignoring yield corridors on metro-linked periphery. Stress-test FX, tax residency, and exit buyer pool before choosing between long-term lease, STR, or lifestyle-primary strategies on the same ticket size.

Rent when:

- First 12 months in Italy on a new visa.
- Your employer may relocate you again within two years.
- You need flexibility between Milan, Rome, and coastal months.
- You have not completed [due diligence](/guides/due-diligence-italy-property/) on a specific building.

Buy when:

- Permesso renewed once and income is stable.
- Monthly rent exceeds 1.2% of comparable purchase price (rough breakeven signal).
- You want to fix housing costs before further Milan or Rome appreciation.
- You can carry 10% to 15% closing costs on top of deposit.

## 183-day tax residency rule and nomad planning

Italian tax residency generally triggers if you spend 183 or more days in Italy in a calendar year, or if your center of vital interests (family, primary business ties, habitual home) sits in Italy. Residents face IRPEF progressive tax on worldwide employment income unless an exemption applies.

High-earning nomads who were non-resident for nine of the past ten years may evaluate the [Italy flat tax regime for new residents](/guides/italy-flat-tax-regime-new-residents/), which replaces foreign-source tax with a €100,000 to €200,000 annual lump sum for up to 15 years. Italian-source salary never enters that lump sum; remote work for foreign employers may qualify as foreign-source depending on treaty analysis and where the work is performed.

| Days in Italy | Likely tax status | Remote work income treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Under 183, vital interests abroad | Non-resident | Usually taxed abroad per treaty |
| 183+ days | Resident | Worldwide reporting to Agenzia delle Entrate |
| Resident + flat tax election | Resident, special regime | Foreign income in lump sum if eligible |

Always confirm treaty position between Italy and your home country with a cross-border commercialista before you buy based on tax assumptions.

## Due diligence for nomad buyers

Nomad buyers skip steps when rushing purchases before permesso renewal. Minimum checks:

2. Fiber point in unit verified with provider.
3. Soundproofing for historic buildings with trams or nightlife.
4. Reciprocity clearance on the rogito for your nationality.
5. IMU and TARI quotes from seller or commercialista.

## Red flags for digital nomad property deals

- Buying before visa approval without understanding Schengen overstays.
- Elective Residence filing with remote salary (automatic rejection).
- Centro Storico flats with no elevator and poor broadband.
- Condo bans on short lets when your plan depends on Airbnb income.
- Ignoring 183-day rule while invoicing Italian clients (creates PE risk).

> Insider tip: Ask the seller for the last two years of condominium minutes before offer. Milan buildings increasingly restrict short lets and Airbnb key boxes, which destroys nomad sublet strategies.

## Closing verification checklist

Use this Italy buyer checklist before compromesso signature or any wire to a notaio escrow account. Your independent avvocato should verify every line; the selling agent does not represent you at rogito.

**Identity and tax setup.** Confirm codice fiscale is issued and matches passport spelling exactly. Non-EU buyers need MAECI reciprocity confirmation in writing from the notaio file before deposit release. Open an Italian bank account or confirm your foreign bank can wire large euro sums with documented purpose codes.

**Title and cadastral consistency.** Request visura catastale storica and compare room counts, surface area, and category class against the on-site geometra survey. Mismatches between APE certificates, listing floor plans, and cadastral maps trigger registration delays and tax reassessments. Search for abusi edilizi (unauthorised works) on pools, terraces, and roof conversions; regularisation fines can exceed €10,000 on rural stock.

**Condominium and municipal debts.** Obtain certificato di regolarità contributi from the building administrator and verify no pending condominium lawsuits. Check IMU arrears and municipal service charges with the Comune; unpaid debts can transfer to the buyer after rogito.

**Rental and licensing.** If income matters, verify CIN (Codice Identificativo Nazionale) transferability or obtain a new CIN before marketing. Read condominium regolamento for tourist-use bans. Confirm SCIA or CIL filings for any structural work completed in the last five years. Model cedolare secca at 21% long-term or 26% short-term on gross rent, not net screenshots from listing portals.

**Closing economics.** Budget registration tax at 9% on second homes (2% only with prima casa and residency registration within 18 months). Add notary fees of 1% to 2%, agent commission if applicable, geometra survey, and mortgage bank costs. Wire only to the notaio conto provvisorio; never to seller personal accounts.

**Exit planning.** Italian plusvalenza may apply on sales within five years of purchase. Keep F24 payment receipts, renovation invoices, and rogito copies for capital gains calculations in Italy and your home country. If you hold through a company structure, confirm annual corporate filings and IVAFE exposure before year-end.

**Remote purchase.** Apostilled power of attorney must match the exact property description in compromesso. Allow two to three weeks for consular legalisation if buying from abroad. Schedule rogito only after mortgage perizia approval and abusi clearance are documented in writing.

**Digital nomad-specific checks.** Run a live fiber speed test at the property during two peak evening windows before compromesso. Confirm the address matches utility bills you will present at the Questura for permesso renewal. If you plan subletting while traveling, verify condominium minutes allow furnished mid-term lets and that your visa category permits the rental model you intend. Non-EU nomads should align purchase timing with Digital Nomad Visa filing so the registered address on the deed matches consulate forms. EU nomads still need codice fiscale and IMU registration even without a national visa. Budget one full business day at the Comune for residency paperwork after rogito if you intend to register within the first month. Store PDF copies of every utility activation receipt in the same folder you use for visa renewal documents. That single folder should also hold your APE certificate, condominium regolamento, and latest internet service contract copy.
