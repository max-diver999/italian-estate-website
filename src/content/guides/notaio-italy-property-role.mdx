# Role of a Notaio in Italy Property Purchases: 2026

The Italian notary, known as the *notaio*, is a mandatory public official appointed by the Ministry of Justice who oversees all property transactions in Italy. The buyer chooses and pays the notary, whose fees range from 1% to 2.5% of the property value. The notary verifies titles, collects registration taxes, and registers the final sale agreement (*rogito*).

## Who is the Italian Notaio (Notary Public)?

An Italian notary is a highly qualified legal professional and public official who is legally empowered by the Italian state to guarantee the validity of real estate contracts. Unlike notary publics in many other countries, the Italian notary represents the neutral public interest, ensuring that every transaction complies with all national laws.

The Italian notary operates as a neutral representative of the Italian state, meaning that the notary public cannot advocate for the specific interests of either the property buyer or the property seller. This requirement of absolute neutrality distinguishes the Italian notary from legal practitioners in common law jurisdictions, who typically serve as partisan representatives for their respective clients. Because of this legal status, the notary's office conducts all transactions with strict objectivity, ensuring that neither party is disadvantaged by unlawful contract clauses or fraudulent terms. Real estate purchasers must realize that while the notary guarantees the absolute legality of the transaction, the public official does not verify the physical condition of the property or negotiate commercial terms. Consequently, foreign buyers should secure their own legal representation to work alongside the notary throughout the [buy property in Italy as a foreigner](/guides/buy-property-italy-foreigner/) process.

The rigorous qualifications required to become a registered Italian notary ensure that these professionals possess an exceptional level of legal expertise. Candidates must hold a university degree in law and complete an intensive eighteen-month internship, followed by passing a highly competitive national examination administered by the Ministry of Justice. The Italian government limits the total number of practicing notaries in each district, meaning that each professional holds a prestigious, state-regulated position within the community. This intensive training prepares the notary to take personal, lifelong responsibility for the absolute legal correctness of every contract signed in their presence. If a notary registers a deed that contains an avoidable legal error, that individual is personally liable for financial damages. This personal liability drives the extreme thoroughness of the notary's investigations.

## Key Legal Duties of the Notaio in Property Sales

The key duties of the Italian notary include performing exhaustive title searches, verifying the buyer's reciprocity status, collecting registration taxes, and registering the deed of sale. The notary acts as the ultimate guarantor of the transaction, ensuring that the property transfer is legally flawless and fully recorded in the public land registry.

The pre-closing title search constitutes one of the most critical responsibilities performed by the notary's office. Under Italian law, the notary must trace the property's ownership history back at least twenty years to ensure that the seller has the undisputed right to sell the property. This extensive investigation, known as the *visura ipotecaria*, checks for any active mortgages, outstanding judicial liens, or third-party claims that could threaten the buyer's future ownership. If the notary uncovers any legal encumbrances during this search, the notary will refuse to execute the final purchase agreement until the seller completely resolves the issue. This search provides the buyer with absolute legal security regarding the title. However, the notary does not verify whether the property's physical structures comply with local planning regulations, which is why a separate surveyor is essential.

In addition to title verification, the notary is legally required to check the buyer's reciprocity rights, particularly for non-European Union citizens. The notary consults the official tables maintained by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs to confirm that the buyer's home country allows Italian citizens to purchase real estate under similar conditions. Once reciprocity is established, the notary prepares the final deed of sale, known as the *rogito*, ensuring all standard clauses match the agreements previously reached by the parties. After the deed is signed, the notary collects all applicable purchase taxes directly from the buyer and is personally responsible for transferring these funds to the Italian tax agency. The notary then completes the process by registering the transaction in the land registry, ensuring that foreign citizens are fully authorized [can foreigners buy property in Italy](/guides/can-foreigners-buy-property-italy/) without restrictions.

| **Post-Closing Phase** | Tax Collection and Registration | Collects purchase taxes from the buyer, registers the deed, and updates the land registry |

## Notaio Fees and Professional Costs Breakdown

Italian notary fees typically range from 1% to 2.5% of the property's purchase price, with the percentage decreasing as the property value increases. These professional fees are paid entirely by the buyer and are separate from the government registration taxes that the notary collects on behalf of the state.

The calculation of notary fees in Italy is based on national guidelines that factor in the property's declared purchase price, the complexity of the contract, and whether the buyer requires an Italian mortgage. For transactions involving a mortgage, the notary must draft a separate mortgage deed, which incurs an additional professional fee. Buyers should expect the notary's professional invoice to include twenty-two percent value-added tax on top of the base fee. In addition to the professional fee, the buyer must provide the notary with the funds required to pay the state registration tax, cadastral tax, and mortgage tax. These fees constitute a key component of the overall [cost of buying property in Italy](/guides/cost-of-buying-property-italy/) which every purchaser must budget for. Because the notary acts as a tax collector, the total amount paid to the notary's office is often much larger than the notary's actual professional fee, making a detailed preliminary quote essential.

To minimize transaction costs, buyers can utilize the *prezzo-valore* system, which allows the notary to calculate purchase taxes based on the property's cadastral value rather than the higher commercial purchase price. This system is available to individual buyers purchasing residential properties, provided that the buyer declares the full transaction price in the deed. When applying the *prezzo-valore* system, the registration tax for a primary residence is reduced to two percent of the cadastral value, while the tax for a second home is nine percent. The notary's professional fees are also legally discounted by thirty percent under this system, providing significant savings for qualifying buyers. Purchasers should discuss this option with their lawyer and notary early in the transaction to ensure all legal conditions are met before closing.

## The Notary Escrow Account (Deposito Prezzo) Explained

The notary escrow account, known as the *deposito prezzo*, is an official escrow service that allows buyers to deposit the purchase price with the notary until the deed is registered. This secure procedure protects the buyer's funds from potential liens or claims against the seller during the registration window.

The *deposito prezzo* service was introduced by Italian Law 124/2017 as a voluntary measure to eliminate the financial risks associated with the gap between signing the deed and its official registration. Under standard procedures, the seller receives the purchase funds at the closing table, but the notary may take several days to register the transaction in the land registry. During this brief window, a creditor could theoretically register a mortgage or lien against the property, which would then bind the new buyer. By using the *deposito prezzo* escrow service, the buyer's funds are held securely in a dedicated, state-protected bank account managed by the notary. The notary only releases the funds to the seller once the land registry confirms that the property has been successfully registered in the buyer's name, providing complete financial security.

This escrow service is particularly beneficial for foreign buyers who are unfamiliar with the Italian legal system or are purchasing property from sellers facing financial difficulties. The escrow funds are legally segregated from the notary's personal assets and are completely immune to any claims from the notary's creditors or the seller's creditors. While the seller must wait a few days to receive their funds, the seller is guaranteed to receive them once registration is complete. The cost of setting up a *deposito prezzo* is minimal, typically ranging from three hundred to five hundred euros in administrative fees. Given the high level of protection this service offers, independent legal experts recommend that international buyers request the *deposito prezzo* escrow service for every Italian property purchase, regardless of the transaction's size.

## The Closing Day (Rogito): What Happens at the Notary Office

The closing day (*rogito*) is the final meeting at the notary's office where the deed of sale is read aloud, signed, and registered. The notary verifies the identities of all parties, explains all legal clauses, witnesses the final payment, and officially transfers ownership of the property.

The *rogito* meeting represents the formal culmination of the property purchase process and must be conducted in the physical presence of the notary. The notary is legally required to read the entire deed of sale aloud in Italian to ensure that both the buyer and the seller fully understand every contractual obligation and financial term. If the buyer does not speak fluent Italian, Italian law mandates that the deed must be prepared in a bilingual format, usually Italian and English, and a certified translator must attend the meeting. This meeting represents the final stage of the [how to buy Italy property step-by-step](/guides/how-to-buy-italy-property-step-by-step/) purchasing journey. This strict requirement prevents any future disputes regarding misunderstandings of the contract. The reading of the deed allows the notary to explain any complex legal terms, such as cadastral classifications, planning history, and tax declarations, ensuring the entire procedure remains transparent and legally compliant.

Once the reading is completed and all parties express their agreement, the buyer transfers the remaining purchase balance, the notary's professional fees, and the government taxes. The buyer, the seller, and any required translators then sign the deed, followed by the notary, who seals the document with their official state stamp. After the meeting, the notary retains the original deed in their official archives and begins the process of registering the transfer with the land registry and the tax office. This registration must be completed within thirty days of the closing meeting, although most professional notaries complete it within a few days. The buyer receives a certified copy of the registered deed once the land registry updates are finalized, which serves as the official proof of the buyer's new ownership of the Italian property.

## Why You Still Need an Independent Real Estate Lawyer

An independent real estate lawyer is essential because the notary is a neutral public official who cannot negotiate terms or protect your individual interests. Your lawyer represents you exclusively, conducting pre-contractual due diligence, negotiating the preliminary agreement (*compromesso*), and drafting protective clauses.

International buyers often mistakenly assume that because the Italian notary is a legal professional, hiring an independent lawyer is unnecessary. However, the notary's legal obligation is to the Italian state and the legal system, not to either party in the transaction. The notary will ensure that the contract is legally valid and that all taxes are paid, but the notary will not advise you on whether the purchase price is fair or if the contract terms are disadvantageous to your interests. For example, if a preliminary agreement lacks protective suspensive clauses regarding mortgage approval or planning compliance, the notary is under no obligation to suggest adding them. An independent real estate lawyer represents you exclusively, negotiating the terms of the preliminary contract (*compromesso*) and ensuring your financial deposits are fully protected against any seller default.

In addition to legal contract review, the notary does not physically inspect the property to verify its actual physical and structural compliance with local planning and building regulations. The notary relies on the seller's formal declaration in the deed that the property is compliant, but the notary does not verify if this declaration is factually accurate. If the property contains illegal building modifications, the new buyer inherits these violations and the associated financial liabilities upon signing the deed. Your independent lawyer, working in coordination with a qualified local surveyor (*Geometra*), will actively investigate the physical compliance of the property before you sign any binding agreements. This independent team ensures that any structural issues or planning discrepancies are resolved by the seller before the closing, as described in our [due diligence for Italian property](/guides/due-diligence-italy-property/) guide, protecting you from unexpected regularization costs or demolition orders after the purchase is finalized.

## Buyer Scenarios and Notary Decision Framework

The notary's role adapts to different buyer profiles and transaction structures, including cash purchases, bank-financed transactions, and remote closings. Understanding these scenarios helps buyers prepare the correct legal documentation and choose the most secure closing method for their specific purchase in Italy.

In a cash purchase scenario involving a non-resident buyer, the notary's role focus is primarily on title verification, reciprocity checks, and tax collection. Since no mortgage bank is involved, the transaction is relatively straightforward, but the buyer faces the risk of paying the seller before the deed is registered. In this scenario, utilizing the notary's escrow account (*deposito prezzo*) is highly recommended to protect the buyer's cash from any intervening claims during the registration window. For buyers securing an Italian mortgage, the notary must coordinate with the bank's legal team and draft two separate deeds on closing day: the property transfer deed (*rogito*) and the mortgage deed (*mutuo*). This double transaction increases the notary's professional fees and requires the notary to register the bank's charge against the property in the land registry simultaneously with the transfer of ownership.

For buyers who cannot attend the closing meeting in person, the notary's role involves verifying a power of attorney, known as a *Procura Speciale*. The buyer can grant this authority to their independent lawyer, who can then sign the final deed on the buyer's behalf at the notary's office. The notary must meticulously verify that the power of attorney document has been executed correctly, apostilled or legalized in the buyer's home country, and translated into Italian by a certified translator. The notary ensures that the representative operates strictly within the limits of the granted authority, protecting both the buyer and the seller from potential legal disputes. This remote option allows international buyers to complete their Italian property purchases without the need for international travel, provided that all legal authorizations are prepared in advance.
