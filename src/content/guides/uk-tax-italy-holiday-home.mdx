# UK Tax on Italy Holiday Home: 2026 Owner Guide

**Quick answer:** If you are UK tax resident, HMRC taxes your Italian holiday home on two timelines. While you own it, report rental profit on SA105 and pay Italian cedolare secca at 21% or 26% plus annual IMU. When you sell, UK CGT applies on the sterling gain at 18% or 24% with no lettings relief on a pure second home. The UK-Italy double tax treaty gives credit for Italian tax already paid. Post-April 2025 reforms ended new remittance basis claims.

For purchase mechanics start with [buying property in Italy from the UK](/guides/italy-property-for-uk-buyers/). For Italian-side annual levies see [IMU property tax in Italy](/guides/imu-property-tax-italy/).

## What UK tax applies when you own an Italian holiday home?

UK tax on an Italy holiday home splits into annual income tax on rent and capital gains tax on sale. Italian layers run in parallel: cedolare secca on rent, IMU on ownership, and plusvalenza on certain disposals within five years. Neither country waits for the other. You file in both jurisdictions and use treaty relief to net the overlap.

The starting point is UK tax residence, not passport or property location. If HMRC treats you as resident in the United Kingdom for a tax year, you are within the worldwide income scope unless a specific exemption applies. Owning a Tuscan farmhouse or a Puglia apartment does not by itself create Italian tax residency, but it does create Italian source income and asset reporting obligations when you let the property or sell it.

Most British holiday-home owners buy under the Italian second-home regime with 9% registration tax and full IMU, as explained in the [Prima Casa vs second home tax comparison](/guides/italy-prima-casa-vs-second-home-tax/). That Italian classification does not change UK treatment. For HMRC the asset is overseas investment or lifestyle property unless you genuinely relocate and evidence main-home status in Italy, which is rare for ninety-day Schengen holiday patterns.

This guide is informational. UK and Italian tax law change frequently. Cross-border ownership needs a UK chartered tax adviser and an Italian commercialista who understand both SA105 reporting and cedolare secca elections.

## How do UK and Italian tax layers stack on the same asset?

UK and Italian tax operate on different triggers. Italy taxes source income at flat rates for non-resident landlords. The UK taxes the same economic profit again through Self Assessment, then allows credit for Italian tax under the Convention between the United Kingdom and Italy for the Avoidance of Double Taxation with respect to Taxes on Income and on Capital.

The table below maps the main levies a UK-resident non-resident Italian owner typically faces on a second home used partly for personal holidays and partly for letting.

## How do you report Italian rental income on HMRC Self Assessment?

Report Italian holiday let income on supplementary pages SA105 (foreign property) of your Self Assessment tax return. Include gross rent converted to sterling, deduct allowable expenses, declare the resulting profit, and claim Foreign Tax Credit Relief for Italian cedolare secca paid in the same tax year.

HMRC expects worldwide rental income even when Italy already withheld or you paid cedolare secca via F24. The workflow is not optional for UK residents who exceed the personal allowance on total income or who are required to file for other reasons.

**Step 1, Gross rent in sterling.** Convert euro rent using HMRC's published average exchange rate for the tax year unless your adviser elects spot rates consistently. Mixed-method conversion triggers enquiries.

**Step 2, Allowable expenses.** Deduct costs wholly attributable to letting: property management commission, platform fees, cleaning between guests, letting insurance, repairs (not capital improvements), and the letting share of IMU. Personal-use periods are excluded from expense allocation unless the property is genuinely available to let throughout.

**Step 3, Italian flat tax.** Cedolare secca is paid in Italy on gross rent without expense deductions. That is efficient locally but creates a mismatch: UK rules allow expenses while Italy taxed gross. Treaty credit usually references tax paid in Italy, not theoretical IRPEF.

**Step 4, UK profit and credit.** Compute UK property profit on SA105. Calculate UK tax on that profit. Claim credit up to the lower of Italian tax paid and UK tax attributable to the same income. Residual UK tax may still be due if your marginal UK rate exceeds the Italian flat rate.

**Step 5, Record keeping.** Retain CIN registration proof for short-term lets, Italian rental contracts, F24 payment receipts, bank statements, and SA105 working papers for at least five UK tax years.

Cedolare secca rates depend on contract structure. Long-term residential leases at 21% compete well against progressive IRPEF up to 43%. Tourist lets at 26% still often beat uncapped IRPEF for high earners but must be weighed against UK marginal rates above 40%. ## What is cedolare secca and when should a UK owner elect it?

Cedolare secca is Italy's optional flat substitute tax on rental income. Non-resident UK owners letting an Italian holiday home typically elect 21% on qualifying long-term contracts or 26% on short-term tourist income instead of progressive IRPEF.

The election is per contract. You cannot mix IRPEF and cedolare secca on the same lease. Once elected, you generally cannot deduct Italian maintenance against that rent for Italian purposes, which simplifies Italian filing but pushes all expense value to the UK SA105 side.

For a UK higher-rate taxpayer near 40% marginal income tax, Italian 26% cedolare secca plus a full foreign tax credit often leaves residual UK tax on any remaining profit after SA105 expenses. Basic-rate UK taxpayers may see Italian tax fully creditable with minimal UK top-up.

Short-term operators must register CIN, comply with regional night caps, and collect tourist tax where municipal rules require it. Failure to register does not remove UK reporting duty; it creates Italian penalties that compound cross-border risk.

Worked example (illustrative, not personalised advice):

- Gross tourist rent: €24,000 in a UK tax year
- Italian cedolare secca at 26%: €6,240
- UK allowable expenses including IMU share and management: €7,000
- UK net profit before credit: €17,000 converted to sterling
- UK tax at illustrative 40% marginal rate on property slice: credit capped by treaty mechanics against €6,240 Italian tax

Run net yield only after both layers using [how to calculate rental yield in Italy](/guides/how-to-calculate-rental-yield-italy/) and your UK adviser's computation.

## Does IMU affect UK tax on your Italian second home?

IMU is Italy's annual municipal property tax on second homes. It is not creditable as foreign tax against UK income tax in the same way as cedolare secca, but the portion attributable to letting is usually deductible when calculating UK rental profit on SA105.

Personal-use months create allocation questions. If you occupy the villa for eight weeks and let it for forty weeks, only the letting share of IMU belongs in SA105 expenses. HMRC may challenge a 100% deduction on a property marketed as a holiday home with heavy owner use.

IMU is calculated from cadastral rent, not market value. Typical second-home bills range from roughly €800 to €3,500 annually on mainstream apartments and small villas, higher in prime Milan or Florence municipalities. Luxury cadastral categories still pay IMU even if someone mistakenly claimed Prima Casa at purchase.

Pay IMU via F24 by 16 June and 16 December. Late payment triggers Italian penalties independent of UK filing status. Non-payment can eventually produce hypothec on the property under Italian law.

## How does UK CGT apply when you sell an Italian holiday home?

UK capital gains tax applies when a UK-resident owner disposes of an Italian residential property for more than base cost plus allowable enhancement spend. Gains above the annual exempt amount are taxed at 18% or 24% for residential property depending on total taxable income level.

Base cost includes purchase price in sterling at acquisition (or consistent conversion policy), Italian registration tax, notary fees, agency commission, and capital improvements with invoices. It excludes repair costs already deducted against rent. Keep the rogito and all F24 payment slips from purchase.

Private Residence Relief in the UK applies only when the property was your main home under UK statutory tests. A pure Italian holiday home rarely qualifies. Lettings relief was sharply restricted from April 2020 and abolished for most holiday-let disposals where the owner does not share occupancy with the tenant. Do not model lettings relief on a Tuscan second home you visit each summer.

Reporting routes:

- **Real-time CGT service:** Report and pay within sixty days of completion if you are UK resident and the property is not covered by annual Self Assessment timing exceptions your adviser confirms.
- **Self Assessment:** Include disposal on SA108 if reporting through the annual return.

Italian plusvalenza may run in parallel if you sell within five years of purchase without exemption. The notary may withhold 26% substitute tax on the gain. Credit that against UK CGT on the same gain element under the treaty, subject to documentation and timing rules your adviser applies.

FX movement can dominate the sterling gain even when euro value is flat. Buying at GBP/EUR 0.86 and selling at 0.90 can reduce sterling gain or create a sterling loss while euro economics look positive. Track both currencies from compromesso deposit through rogito sale.

## What changed for remittance basis owners from April 2025?

The UK abolished the remittance basis for UK-resident non-domiciled individuals from 6 April 2025. New remittance basis claims were not available from the 2025-26 tax year onward. Pre-April 2025 foreign income and gains may still be taxed when remitted to the UK under transitional rules.

If you previously used the remittance basis, three 2026-aware points matter for Italian property:

**Arising basis default.** From 2025-26 most former remittance-basis users declare foreign income when it arises, including Italian rent credited to an offshore euro account. Bringing rent to a UK bank no longer defines the charge alone; worldwide basis expansion is the default.

**Transitional foreign income relief.** For 2025-26 only, certain former remittance-basis users who do not qualify for the new four-year foreign income and gains regime pay UK tax on 50% of foreign income (not foreign chargeable gains). This is a one-year bridge, not a permanent holiday-home exemption.

**Temporary Repatriation Facility (TRF).** For tax years 2025-26 through 2027-28, eligible individuals may remit pre-6 April 2025 foreign income and gains at reduced rates of 12% in the first two years and 15% in the third year under designation elections. TRF does not eliminate UK tax on new Italian rent earned after April 2025.

**Four-year FIG regime.** Individuals who become UK tax resident after ten consecutive non-UK tax years may qualify for a four-year exemption on foreign income and gains arising in those first four years, with freedom to remit those sums to the UK. Italian rental income earned during that window may be exempt in the UK while still taxable in Italy under cedolare secca unless treaty analysis says otherwise. Eligibility is fact-specific.

Italian property purchased with offshore funds before April 2025 does not automatically stay outside UK CGT on disposal. CGT rebasing rules for long-term remittance-basis users who dispose of foreign assets may reference 5 April 2017 or 5 April 2019 values depending on election history. This is high-stakes planning territory, not a blog summary.

## How does the UK-Italy double tax treaty reduce double taxation?

The UK-Italy double tax treaty allocates taxing rights and provides relief when the same income or gain could be taxed twice. Rental income may be taxed in Italy as source state and in the UK as residence state, with credit given for tax paid in Italy against UK liability on the same income.

For capital gains on immovable property, the treaty generally permits Italy to tax gains on Italian real estate while the UK also taxes its residents on worldwide gains. Relief is by credit, not by exemption. The practical outcome is one combined effective rate near the higher of the two systems after credit mechanics, not double full taxation.

Treaty relief does not remove filing duties. You still complete SA105 and SA108, declare Italian tax paid, and attach computations. Automatic exchange of information between Italy and the UK via CRS means undeclared Italian rent increasingly surfaces in HMRC enquiries.

National Insurance and Italian social charges on letting are separate from income tax treaty articles. Managed STR setups through Italian companies trigger additional treaty and permanent establishment questions beyond this guide.

## Insider tip: match your UK tax year to Italian F24 evidence

The most common SA105 failure among British Italy owners is not underpaying cedolare secca, it is mismatching tax years. HMRC measures rent on 6 April to 5 April. Italy often issues F24 cedolare payments on calendar-year cash flows or contract anniversaries. An inspector seeing €12,000 rent on SA105 but €8,000 Italian tax credits in the same UK year will ask for a reconciliation schedule.

Build a simple spreadsheet with three columns: date, euro amount, and purpose (rent received, cedolare paid, IMU paid, capital expense). Convert at one HMRC-approved method per year. Give your UK adviser Italian quietanze before 31 January, not after the deadline rush. If you use an Italian gestore for STR, request an annual riepilogo aligned to your UK year even when their default is calendar year.

This ten-minute discipline usually saves more than aggressive expense claims on personal utility bills that mix owner holidays with letting.

## Buyer Scenarios and Decision Framework

### Scenario 1: UK employed higher-rate owner, long-term let in Umbria (€380,000 purchase)

Profile: Manchester-based IT director, UK tax resident, lets a two-bedroom apartment ten months per year under a four-year contract at €1,100 monthly.

Italian stack: 21% cedolare secca on €13,200 gross rent = €2,772. IMU roughly €1,400. Net Italian cash tax before management roughly €4,200.

UK stack: SA105 with management and IMU deductions might show €6,500 sterling equivalent profit. Foreign tax credit for €2,772 cedolare. Residual UK tax at 40% marginal slice on remaining profit after credit.

Decision rule: Long-term cedolare at 21% plus disciplined SA105 expenses often beats uncapped IRPEF. Compare against personal use plans in [Italy property for UK buyers](/guides/italy-property-for-uk-buyers/).

### Scenario 2: STR investor in Puglia, basic-rate UK taxpayer (€290,000 purchase)

Profile: Bristol couple, tourist lettings one hundred twenty nights at €130 average nightly rate, professional manager at 25%.

Italian stack: 26% cedolare on €15,600 gross = €4,056. CIN and municipal tourist tax compliance mandatory. IMU share allocated 70% to letting.

UK stack: Lower marginal rate means foreign tax credit may cover most UK liability on net SA105 profit if expenses are robust. Model gross-to-net in [gross vs net yield Italy](/guides/gross-vs-net-yield-italy/) before purchase.

Decision rule: STR tax at 26% still works for basic-rate UK owners when occupancy exceeds seventy nights and management costs are contractually transparent.

### Scenario 3: Former remittance-basis user selling Ligurian flat within four years

Profile: London non-dom transitioning to arising basis 2025-26, bought in 2023, selling 2026 with €85,000 euro gain.

Italian stack: Plusvalenza likely due at 26% substitute tax on gain unless primary residence exemption documented (unlikely on holiday pattern).

UK stack: CGT at 18% or 24% on sterling gain with credit for Italian tax. No lettings relief. Check TRF only for pre-2025 offshore sale proceeds remittance, not for the gain calculation itself.

Decision rule: Delay sale beyond five-year Italian window if Italian plusvalenza dominates; coordinate with UK CGT payment deadline and notary withholding.

### Scenario 4: New UK returnee qualifying for four-year FIG regime, Florence apartment

Profile: British citizen returned to UK after twelve years in Dubai, buys €620,000 Florence second home, occasional STR.

Italian stack: Full cedolare secca and IMU apply locally regardless of UK FIG status.

UK stack: Potential exemption on foreign income and gains during first four UK tax years if FIG election valid; Italian rent may still need treaty analysis. Do not assume FIG exempts future CGT on disposal after regime ends.

Decision rule: FIG is immigration and residence sequencing, not a substitute for Italian commercialista filings. Align with [buy property in Italy as a foreigner](/guides/buy-property-italy-foreigner/) compliance first.

## What records should you keep for HMRC and Agenzia delle Entrate?

Maintain one digital vault with: rogito and compromesso; codice fiscale; cadastral visura; IMU F24 for each instalment; cedolare secca F24; CIN certificate; rental contracts; platform annual statements; renovation invoices flagged capital vs repair; UK SA105 and SA108 workings; and FX conversion rate source per tax year.

Italian agents sometimes issue gross rent statements without splitting owner-use weeks. Add a calendar export from Airbnb or your gestore showing available-to-let nights. HMRC allocation disputes on mixed-use properties are won on contemporaneous calendars, not retroactive estimates.

If you inherit Italian property, UK and Italian inheritance rules intersect with a fresh base cost for CGT purposes. See [Italy inheritance law for foreigners](/guides/italy-inheritance-law-property-foreigners/) before planning disposal within five years of succession.
