# Italy Golden Visa & Investor Visa: 2026 Property Guide

The Italian Investor Visa — widely searched as the **Italy golden visa** — is a residency-by-investment pathway for non-EU nationals who commit €250,000 to €2,000,000 to regulated financial categories. It does **not** allow property alone to qualify, but most investors still [buy property in Italy](/guides/buy-property-italy-foreigner/) for accommodation, lifestyle, or rental income. This guide maps investment thresholds, the 3-month execution deadline, property alignment, and how the program differs from elective residence and flat-tax relocation.

## What are Italian investor visa requirements for non-EU applicants?

Non-EU applicants need valid passport, clean criminal records (apostilled), proof of legal source of funds, CV, health insurance, accommodation proof, and commitment to one of four investment categories. The Nulla Osta portal review targets 30 days; consular visa issuance follows within six months of approval. After entry, Questura registration within eight days and full capital transfer within three months are mandatory — missing the transfer window revokes the permit.

Corporate structures need visura camerale and anti-shell verification for the €500,000 company route.

## How does Italy residency by investment work if you also buy property?

Residency by investment grants a two-year permesso di soggiorno per investitori, renewable for three-year periods while the underlying investment remains compliant. Property purchase does not fund the visa but satisfies accommodation evidence and supports lifestyle or rental strategy. Investors often sequence: (1) select investment category and open Italian bank compliance, (2) apply Nulla Osta, (3) sign compromesso on target home, (4) enter Italy, register Questura, execute bond/startup transfer, (5) complete rogito with notaio.

Italian-sourced rental income faces standard IRPEF or cedolare secca; foreign income may qualify for the Article 24-bis flat-tax regime if the investor elects tax residency — compare pathways in [flat tax vs investor visa Italy](/compare/flat-tax-vs-investor-visa-italy/). Elective residence remains the alternative for passive-income retirees who accept 183-day stay rules — see [elective residence vs investor visa](/compare/elective-residence-vs-investor-visa-italy/).


The Italian Investor Visa is a fast-track residency program granting non-EU citizens a 2-year permit in exchange for a minimum financial commitment of €250,000 in innovative startups, €500,000 in local companies, or €2,000,000 in government bonds. The application process is fully digitalized and typically takes under 3 months to complete.

The Italian government established this residency-by-investment scheme under Legislative Decree 286/1998, specifically Article 26-bis, to attract foreign capital and stimulate key sectors of the domestic economy. Unlike other European residency programs that have faced retrenchment or outright cancellation, the Italian program remains highly stable, welcoming, and legally secure. The program is specifically tailored for high-net-worth individuals, entrepreneurs, and global executives who require unrestricted access to the Schengen Area without the burden of mandatory physical relocation.

The initial residence permit (permesso di soggiorno per investitori) is valid for 2 years. The foreign investor can renew the permit for subsequent 3-year periods, provided the underlying investment remains fully intact and active within the Italian territory. A key advantage of this pathway is the absence of any language proficiency requirements or minimum physical stay obligations during the initial residency phase. The foreign investor can maintain global business operations while holding a prestigious European residency status.

The administrative framework is managed by an interministerial committee, which coordinates the evaluation of applications through a dedicated digital portal. This centralized approach eliminates regional bureaucratic delays, ensuring that the initial clearance, known as the Nulla Osta, is processed within 30 days of a complete submission. This makes the Italian Investor Visa one of the most efficient and reliable administrative pathways in the European residency market.

## Can a Foreigner Obtain the Italian Investor Visa Through Real Estate?

No, direct real estate purchase is not an eligible investment category for the Italian Investor Visa. However, a foreign investor can buy property separately for personal use while securing residency through one of the 4 eligible financial investment pathways, such as a €250,000 startup investment or a €500,000 company injection.

The Italian legislature intentionally excluded direct real estate acquisitions from the Golden Visa framework to protect the domestic housing market from speculative price inflation. This policy stands in sharp contrast to historic programs in Portugal, Spain, and Greece, where direct property purchases led to significant local displacement and subsequent legislative restrictions. By separating residency qualification from property ownership, Italy ensures that the housing market remains stable while offering a clear, dual-track strategy for affluent buyers.

Under this dual-track approach, the foreign investor secures residency by allocating capital to a productive financial asset, such as government bonds or corporate shares. Simultaneously, the investor proceeds to [buy property in Italy as a foreigner](/guides/buy-property-italy-foreigner/) to establish a luxurious personal residence or a high-yield rental asset. This structure allows the investor to enjoy the security of a regulated financial investment alongside the tangible benefits of Italian real estate ownership.

To satisfy the administrative requirements of the visa, the applicant must demonstrate proof of suitable accommodation in Italy once the residence permit is issued. Acquiring a residential property is the most definitive way to satisfy this requirement. The real estate transaction proceeds under standard Italian property laws, allowing the investor to build a diversified portfolio that combines secure financial instruments with premium Italian real estate.

## What are the Eligible Investment Categories for the Italian Golden Visa?

The Italian Golden Visa offers 4 distinct financial pathways: €250,000 in innovative startups, €500,000 in active limited companies, €1,000,000 in philanthropic donations, or €2,000,000 in government bonds. Each category requires the foreign investor to maintain the full capital amount in Italy for the entire 2-year duration of the initial residency permit.

The selection of the investment category depends on the investor's risk tolerance, financial objectives, and desired level of active management. The 4 pathways are structured as follows:

### 1. Innovative Startups (€250,000)
This pathway represents the lowest entry threshold but carries the highest commercial risk. The investment must be directed toward an enterprise registered in the official section of the Italian Business Register dedicated to "innovative startups" (start-up innovative). These companies operate in high-tech sectors, scientific research, or digital development. The foreign investor must acquire equity shares and maintain the investment for the duration of the residency permit.

### 2. Limited Companies (€500,000)
The investor can allocate capital to an active limited company (Società a Responsabilità Limitata - S.r.l., or Società per Azioni - S.p.A.) incorporated and operating in Italy. This investment can take the form of a capital increase or the purchase of existing shares on the secondary market. The target company must be in good financial standing and have active operations. This option allows investors to target established businesses with stable cash flows or even invest in their own Italian corporate structures.

### 3. Philanthropic Donations (€1,000,000)
This is a non-refundable, public-interest donation directed toward specific projects in culture, education, immigration management, scientific research, or the recovery of cultural and natural heritage. While this option does not offer financial returns, it appeals to philanthropic investors who wish to support Italian cultural preservation while securing residency.

### 4. Government Bonds (€2,000,000)
This is the most conservative and popular pathway for risk-averse investors. The capital must be used to purchase Italian government debt securities, including Buoni del Tesoro Poliennali (BTP), CCT, or CTZ. The bonds must have a remaining maturity of at least 2 years at the time of acquisition and must be held in a dedicated custody account with an Italian financial institution. This option preserves capital while generating predictable, sovereign-backed yields.

The foreign investor must declare the chosen category at the beginning of the application process. The committed capital must originate from bank accounts held in the applicant's name, and the applicant must provide comprehensive documentation detailing the legal source of the funds.

## What is the Step-by-Step Application Timeline for the Italian Investor Visa?

The application timeline spans approximately 3 to 4 months, beginning with the online Nulla Osta request which takes up to 30 days. After receiving the Nulla Osta, the foreign investor has 6 months to apply for the visa at an Italian consulate and must transfer the investment within 3 months of entry.

The application process is divided into 4 distinct phases, each governed by strict legal deadlines and documentation requirements:

### Phase 1: The Nulla Osta Application (Weeks 1-4)
The foreign investor submits the initial application through the dedicated online portal of the Ministry of Enterprises and Made in Italy. The applicant must upload a comprehensive set of documents, including valid identification, a detailed curriculum vitae, a clean criminal record certificate, and documented proof of the legal origin of the funds. The applicant must also sign a formal declaration committing to execute the chosen investment within the prescribed timeframe. The interministerial committee reviews the file and issues the Nulla Osta within 30 days of a complete submission.

### Phase 2: Consular Visa Issuance (Weeks 5-8)
Upon receiving the Nulla Osta, the investor has exactly 6 months to apply for the Investor Visa at the Italian embassy or consulate in their country of residence. The consular office verifies the Nulla Osta and issues the physical visa, which is valid for 2 years and allows multiple entries into the Schengen Area.

### Phase 3: Entry and Questura Declaration (Weeks 9-12)
The investor enters Italy using the visa. Within 8 days of crossing the Italian border, the investor must travel to the local police headquarters (Questura) in the municipality where they intend to reside. The investor submits fingerprints and files the formal application for the physical residence permit (permesso di soggiorno per investitori).

### Phase 4: Execution of the Investment (Within 3 Months)
The investor must complete the full financial transfer of the committed capital to the target startup, company, or government bonds within 3 months of entering Italy or receiving the residence permit. The investor must upload official bank transfer receipts and corporate certificates to the Ministry portal to verify the transaction. The interministerial committee reviews the proof and issues a final validation certificate. Failure to complete the transfer within this 3-month window results in the immediate revocation of the residence permit.

## How Does the Investor Visa Compare to the Elective Residence Visa?

The Investor Visa requires a minimum €250,000 financial commitment but imposes zero physical stay requirements, whereas the Elective Residence Visa requires a passive income of €31,160 annually and obligates the holder to spend at least 183 days per year in Italy, making the Investor Visa far more flexible.

The choice between the Investor Visa and the Elective Residence Visa (ERV) depends on the applicant's financial profile, professional activity, and long-term residency goals. The Elective Residence Visa is designed primarily for retirees and individuals with substantial, passive income from pensions, real estate, or trust funds. It strictly prohibits the holder from engaging in any form of employment, freelance work, or active business management within Italy.

In addition, the ERV requires the applicant to demonstrate a minimum passive income of €31,160 per year, which must be increased by 20% for a spouse and 5% for each dependent child. Because the ERV obligates the holder to spend at least 183 days per year in Italy, it automatically triggers Italian tax residency, subjecting the holder's global income to progressive Italian tax rates (IRPEF) ranging from 23% to 43%, unless specific tax treaties or alternative regimes apply.

By contrast, the Investor Visa offers unparalleled flexibility. It imposes no minimum physical stay requirements, allowing the foreign investor to maintain their primary home and business operations abroad while enjoying unrestricted access to Europe. Additionally, the Investor Visa holder is legally permitted to work, start a business, or manage active investments in Italy. This makes it the ideal choice for active entrepreneurs and global executives.

| Feature | Investor Visa (Golden Visa) | Elective Residence Visa (ERV) | Strategic Advantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Minimum Investment | €250,000 to €2,000,000 | None (passive income only) | ERV has no capital outlay; Investor Visa requires capital |
| Passive Income | None required | €31,160 annually minimum | Investor Visa does not require passive income proof |

The Investor Visa represents a significant capital allocation but provides complete operational and travel freedom, whereas the ERV is a low-cost entry pathway that imposes restrictive lifestyle and tax obligations.

## What are the Tax Implications for Italian Investor Visa Holders?

Italian Investor Visa holders can access a highly attractive flat-tax regime under Article 24-bis, which allows new residents to pay a fixed annual tax of €100,000 on all foreign-sourced income. This tax incentive is valid for up to 15 years and completely exempts foreign assets from local wealth taxes.

The Italian flat-tax regime, introduced under Article 24-bis of the Italian Tax Code (Testo Unico delle Imposte sui Redditi), is one of the most competitive tax incentives in the global residency market. It is specifically designed to attract high-net-worth individuals and their families to Italy. Under this regime, the primary applicant pays a lump-sum tax of €100,000 per year, which covers all foreign-sourced income, including dividends, capital gains, rental income, and business profits generated outside of Italy.

The primary applicant can extend this flat-tax benefit to family members for an additional fixed fee of €25,000 per person annually. This regime provides complete exemption from Italian inheritance and gift taxes on all assets located outside of Italy. It also exempts the holder from standard reporting requirements for foreign assets (Quadru RW) and eliminates local wealth taxes, such as the IVIE (tax on foreign real estate) and IVAFE (tax on foreign financial assets).

To qualify for this regime, the applicant must transfer their tax residency to Italy and must not have been a tax resident of Italy for at least 9 of the 10 years preceding the application. The flat-tax status is valid for a maximum of 15 years and can be revoked by the taxpayer at any time. Any income generated within the Italian territory remains subject to standard progressive Italian tax rates (IRPEF), which range from 23% to 43%. Therefore, the regime is highly beneficial for individuals whose primary wealth and income streams are located outside of Italy.

## What are the Family Reunification Rules Under the Italian Golden Visa?

The main applicant can include their spouse, dependent children under 18 years, and dependent parents over 65 years in the residency application without making any additional financial investments. Family members receive a 2-year residence permit that grants full rights to work, study, and access public healthcare in Italy.

The family reunification framework (ricongiungimento familiare) is governed by Article 29 of the Italian Consolidated Law on Immigration. It allows the primary investor to secure residency for their immediate family members under a single investment application, ensuring that the family can relocate and live together in Italy.

The eligible family members who can be included in the application are:
- The spouse, provided they are at least 18 years of age and legally married or in a recognized civil union.
- Minor children, including adopted or stepchildren, under 18 years of age, who are unmarried and financially dependent on the main applicant.
- Adult dependent children, only if they are unable to provide for their own support due to severe physical or mental disability.
- Dependent parents, provided they do not have other children in their country of origin, or are over 65 years of age and their other children are unable to support them due to documented, serious health reasons.

Family members do not need to make any separate financial investments or purchase additional assets. They receive a residence permit of the same duration as the primary investor (initially 2 years, renewable for 3 years). This permit grants them full legal rights to reside in Italy, enroll in public or private educational institutions, engage in employment or self-employment, and register with the National Health Service (Servizio Sanitario Nazionale - SSN) to access high-quality public healthcare.

## What are the Common Pitfalls and Red Flags for Investors in Italy?

The most critical risk is failing to execute the full financial transfer within 3 months of entering Italy, which results in the immediate revocation of the 2-year residence permit. Investors must also avoid unapproved startup structures or companies that do not meet the strict legal criteria of the Ministry.

Navigating the Italian regulatory and bureaucratic landscape requires meticulous attention to detail. Foreign investors must be aware of several common pitfalls that can jeopardize their application or lead to the revocation of their residency status:

### 1. The 3-Month Investment Deadline
The 3-month window to execute the financial transfer is non-negotiable. The timeline begins the moment the investor enters Italy or receives their physical residence permit. Delays in international wire transfers, bank compliance checks, or source of funds verification can cause investors to miss this deadline, resulting in the immediate cancellation of the visa.

### 2. Ineligible Corporate Structures
The target company for the €500,000 investment must be an active, operating limited company (S.r.l. or S.p.A.) in good standing. Investing in real estate holding companies, shell companies, or inactive corporate entities is strictly prohibited. The interministerial committee conducts thorough audits, and any investment in an unapproved structure will lead to immediate rejection.

### 3. Inadequate Source of Funds Documentation
The Italian Ministry of Enterprises conducts rigorous anti-money laundering (AML) and background checks. The applicant must provide a clear, unbroken paper trail detailing the legal origin of the investment capital. Bank statements, tax returns, corporate audits, and property sale contracts must be translated into Italian or English and legally certified. Any ambiguity or missing documentation will result in prolonged delays or rejection.

### 4. Lack of Independent Local Advice
Relying solely on developers, promoters, or unverified intermediaries is a significant risk. The foreign investor must engage independent legal counsel and a qualified Italian accountant (commercialista) to manage the application, verify the target investment, and coordinate with the Ministry. This independent oversight protects the investor's capital and ensures full compliance with all local laws.

:::tip[Insider Tip: Escrow Accounts and Bank Compliance]
To mitigate the risk of missing the strict 3-month investment window, foreign investors should utilize a dedicated Italian bank account or an escrow account managed by an independent legal professional. Italian banks are subject to stringent European anti-money laundering (AML) regulations, and the compliance onboarding process can take up to 6 weeks. Initiating bank account opening and source of funds verification well before receiving the Nulla Osta is a critical step that prevents administrative delays from jeopardizing the residency permit.
:::

## How Can an Investor Transition to Permanent Residency and Citizenship?

After 5 years of continuous residency under the Investor Visa, a foreign citizen can apply for permanent EU residency, provided the applicant passes an A2 Italian language test. After 10 years of legal residency, the investor can apply for full Italian citizenship through naturalization, securing a powerful passport.

The transition from temporary residency to permanent status and eventual citizenship is a structured process that requires consistent compliance with Italian immigration and tax laws:

### Permanent EU Residency (After 5 Years)
To qualify for the long-term EU residence permit (permesso di soggiorno UE per soggiornanti di lungo periodo), the foreign investor must demonstrate:
- 5 years of continuous, legal residency in Italy. The applicant must not have been absent from Italy for more than 6 consecutive months, or more than 10 months in total over the 5-year period.
- Sufficient financial resources, equal to or greater than the annual social allowance.
- A passing grade in an official A2-level Italian language proficiency exam.
- Proof of suitable housing that complies with local municipal sanitary and safety standards.

Permanent residency grants the holder the right to live, work, and study indefinitely in Italy and other EU member states without the need to renew their residence permit every few years.

### Italian Citizenship through Naturalization (After 10 Years)
Under standard Italian nationality law, a non-EU citizen who has maintained continuous legal residency in Italy for 10 years can apply for citizenship through naturalization. The requirements include:
- 10 years of uninterrupted legal residency, verified through municipal registration records (Anagrafe).
- A clean criminal record in Italy and all previous countries of residence.
- A passing grade in an official B1-level Italian language proficiency exam, demonstrating a solid command of the language.
- Consistent tax compliance and income generation within Italy, unless the applicant is covered by specific high-net-worth tax regimes such as the Article 24-bis flat tax.

Acquiring Italian citizenship grants the investor a powerful European passport, allowing visa-free travel to over 190 countries, full voting rights, and the freedom to live, work, and retire anywhere within the European Union.
