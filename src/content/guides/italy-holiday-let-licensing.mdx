# Italy Holiday Let Licensing: 2026 Short-Term Rental Guide

**Quick answer:** To legally operate a short-term holiday let in Italy, owners must obtain a national Codice Identificativo Nazionale (CIN) and register properties with local authorities. Operating without a CIN triggers severe non-compliance penalties, with fines ranging from €800 to €8,000, while failure to submit local SCIA declarations can result in business suspension and additional fines up to €10,000.

Buying property in Italy with the intention of renting it out to tourists is a highly lucrative strategy, but the days of casual, unregulated hosting are officially over. The Italian government has implemented sweeping legal reforms to unify rental rules, eliminate undeclared income, and professionalize the short-term rental market. 

Understanding these requirements is a fundamental part of your pre-purchase research. Compliance directly influences your operational costs, legal liability, and net returns. Before deciding where to invest, it is critical to align your purchase plans with both federal regulations and specific regional guidelines.

## What Is the Italian National CIN Registration System?

The Codice Identificativo Nazionale (CIN) is Italy's mandatory national registration database system designed to regulate short-term holiday rentals under 30 days. Replacing old regional systems, the CIN must be displayed on all online booking listings and at the property entrance, with non-compliance resulting in steep fines between €800 and €8,000.

### The Background of the National CIN Reform

Introduced under Decree Law No. 145/2023 and fully active nationwide, the CIN was created to combat tax evasion and standardise short-term rentals (locazioni brevi). Historically, each of Italy’s 20 regions operated its own registration system, resulting in massive fragmentation and a lack of federal oversight. 

To resolve this, the Ministry of Tourism established the National Database of Accommodation Facilities (Banca Dati Nazionale delle Strutture Ricettive, or BDSR). The CIN is generated through this central database and serves as proof that the property has met both tax registration and safety standards.

### How the Alphanumeric Code is Structured

Every CIN follows a strict, standardized format that identifies the property's exact location and category. A typical CIN looks like this: IT-015146-LNI-12345-X. 

- IT: The country prefix (Italy).
- 015146: The cadastral municipality code (Codice ISTAT), identifying the city or town.
- LNI: The type of accommodation (e.g., Locazione Turistica or Struttura Ricettiva).
- 12345: A unique five-digit sequential number assigned to the specific unit.
- X: A control letter used for automated verification.

### Advertising and Display Requirements

Once you secure your CIN, you are legally required to display it. The law states that the code must be included in every online and offline listing on online travel agencies (OTAs) like Airbnb, Booking.com, and Vrbo.

It must also be published on your personal direct-booking website and social media promotions, and fixed physically outside the property entrance. This usually means placing a small plaque near the main doorbell, intercom, or entrance mailbox. The code must be clearly legible to bypassers and local inspectors.

## What Is the Difference Between CIR and CIN Codes?

The CIR is a regional identification code issued by local provinces, whereas the CIN is the new unified national identification code linked to a central database. Owners must first secure their regional CIR through local portals before applying for the national CIN, ensuring dual-level compliance with both regional and federal tourism authorities.

### Understanding the Dual-Code Relationship

The introduction of the federal CIN did not entirely eliminate regional tourism authorities. Instead, it created a dual-layered compliance protocol. Local regions still control regional planning, regional statistics, and local tourism policies. 

Therefore, you cannot bypass the regional level to apply directly for a federal CIN. You must first register with your regional portal to obtain a Codice Identificativo Regionale (CIR)—known by different names in some regions, such as IUN in Sardinia or CAV in Tuscany. Once the local province approves your CIR, that data is pushed to the federal BDSR database, allowing you to claim your national CIN.

### Why You Need Both for Full Compliance

Operating with only a regional CIR is no longer legally sufficient. While your CIR proves registration with the local region, the federal CIN is the code checked by online platforms and tax inspectors. If your listing displays a CIR but lacks a CIN, booking portals are legally obligated to deactivate your account.

| Feature | Regional CIR (Codice Identificativo Regionale) | National CIN (Codice Identificativo Nazionale) |
|---|---|---|
| Primary Scope | Local regional planning, municipal statistics, and zoning. | Centralized federal tracking, OTA monitoring, tax compliance. |
| Database System | Managed by individual regional tourism portals. | Managed by the Ministry of Tourism via the BDSR database. |
| Alphanumeric Format | Varies by region (can be numbers, letters, or mixed). | Standardized format beginning with the country code IT. |
| Issuing Authority | Regional administration (Regione) or local province. | Federal Ministry of Tourism (Ministero del Turismo). |
| Local Enforcement | Local municipal police (Polizia Locale) inspections. | Guardia di Finanza, Agenzia delle Entrate, and local police. |
| Booking Platform Rules | Accepted historically, but insufficient under 2026 rules. | Strictly mandatory; platforms block listings without verified CINs. |

## When Is a SCIA Declaration Required for Holiday Lets?

A SCIA (Segnalazione Certificata di Inizio Attività) is required when an owner operates holiday lets as a commercial business, which from 2026 is automatically triggered by managing three or more rental properties. Amateur hosts renting one or two properties occasionally are typically exempt, though some local municipalities require SCIA for all listings.

### Non-Professional vs. Professional Hosts

Italy distinguishes between non-professional (amateur) and professional (commercial) short-term rentals. This distinction is critical because it dictates your tax obligations, accounting costs, and the level of municipal permits required.

- Non-Professional (Amatoriale): This applies to individuals renting out up to two properties. Under this classification, you do not need to register a business, obtain a VAT number (Partita IVA), or file a SCIA in most municipalities. You pay a simplified flat tax on your rental income.
- Professional (Imprenditoriale): From January 1, 2026, the threshold for professional classification was lowered. If you manage three or more short-term rental properties, you are legally deemed a professional hospitality business. You must register a Partita IVA, open a commercial business registry account, file a SCIA, and pay standard corporate or progressive income taxes.

### What is a SCIA and How Do You File It?

The SCIA (Segnalazione Certificata di Inizio Attività) is a formal administrative declaration submitted to the municipal One-Stop Shop for Productive Activities (Sportello Unico per le Attività Produttive, or SUAP). It certifies that your property complies with all urban, building, safety, and health regulations required to operate a commercial accommodation business.

Filing a SCIA must be done digitally. Because it involves complex legal declarations, building certifications, and zoning compliance, most foreign investors hire a local surveyor (geometra) or commercial accountant (commercialista) to prepare and submit the file.

### Required Documentation for Licensing

Securing your CIR, CIN, and SCIA requires a structured set of documents. Preparing this paperwork well in advance is essential to prevent delays in your rental launch.

| Document / Action | Purpose | Issuing Authority | Estimated Processing Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Codice Fiscale | Personal tax identification number required for all applications. | Agenzia delle Entrate | Issued immediately upon application |
| Visura Catastale | Official land registry search proving property details and ownership. | Catasto (Land Registry) | Retrieved online within 24 hours |
| Agibilità Certificate | Certificate proving the property meets health, safety, and structural rules. | Municipal Technical Office (Comune) | Exists with property; copy in 1-2 weeks |
| Regional CIR Application | Local registration to obtain the regional license code. | Regional Tourism Office | 5 to 15 business days |
| National CIN Application | Central database registration linking property to federal registry. | Ministry of Tourism (BDSR Portal) | Automated; 24-48 hours after CIR sync |
| SCIA Filing | Official business commencement declaration (for 3 or more properties). | Municipal SUAP Office | Effective immediately upon digital submission |

### Decision Framework and Compliance Scenarios

To help you determine which rules apply to your specific situation, review these common investor scenarios and buyer profiles:

- Amateur Host Scenario (1-2 properties): If you purchase one or two apartments and rent them occasionally to tourists, you operate under the non-professional regime. No VAT or SCIA is required, and you pay 21% or 26% Cedolare Secca tax on rental revenue.
- Portfolio Investor Scenario (3+ properties): From 2026, managing three or more units automatically triggers professional status. You must register for a Partita IVA, file a SCIA, and maintain commercial accounting, which excludes you from using the flat-rate Cedolare Secca tax regime.
- Managed Service Scenario: If you hire a professional property management company to run your holiday let, they will handle the day-to-day CIN and Alloggiati Web registrations. However, you must verify that the property has a valid municipal SCIA if your portfolio size triggers the business threshold.

## What Are the Safety Requirements for Italian Holiday Lets?

All short-term rental properties in Italy must be equipped with certified fire safety devices, specifically functional fire extinguishers, smoke alarms, and carbon monoxide gas detectors. These safety measures are mandatory for securing a national CIN, and failure to install or maintain them carries penal fines ranging from €600 to €6,000.

### Fire Safety and Detection Standards

Prior to the 2024-2025 reforms, safety standards for private short-term rentals were largely voluntary. Today, fire safety compliance is a strict legal requirement. Under Article 13-ter of DL 145/2023, you cannot obtain a CIN without formally declaring that your property meets the following criteria:

- Certified Fire Extinguishers: You must install certified portable fire extinguishers of at least class 13A-89B. Extinguishers must be placed in visible, accessible locations. The rule requires at least one extinguisher per floor, plus an extinguisher located near high-risk areas like kitchens, heating rooms, or fireplaces.
- Smoke Detectors: Working smoke alarms must be installed in all main living spaces, corridors, and bedrooms. They must be CE-certified and compliant with European standard EN 14604.
- Carbon Monoxide (CO) Alarms: If your property contains any gas appliances—such as a gas boiler, gas stove, gas fireplace, or gas heaters—you are legally required to install a certified carbon monoxide detector. The device must comply with standard EN 50291 and be positioned in the same room as the gas appliance.

### Emergency Information and Maintenance

Beyond hardware installation, owners must display clear emergency information inside the property. This must include a clear, simple evacuation plan printed in both Italian and English, and emergency contact numbers (e.g., 112 for general emergencies, 115 for the fire brigade, 118 for medical emergencies).

Additionally, fire extinguishers must be professionally inspected and serviced every six months by a certified technician. You must maintain a logbook of these inspections at the property, as local police or fire inspectors can demand to see it during random audits.

## How Do Owners Handle Police Registrations and Tourist Taxes?

Property owners must register all overnight guests on the national Alloggiati Web police portal within 24 hours of arrival, or immediately for single-night stays. Additionally, hosts must collect municipal tourist taxes (imposta di soggiorno), which can range from €1.50 to €9.50 per person per night, and remit them to the local city council.

### The Alloggiati Web Registration Process

Under Article 109 of the Consolidated Law on Public Safety (TULPS), registration is a strict national security requirement. You must submit the details of every single guest—including children and infants—to the State Police (Polizia de Stato).

- The Timeline: You must submit guest data within 24 hours of their arrival. If a guest is staying for only one night, their details must be uploaded immediately upon check-in.
- The Procedure: You collect clear photos or scans of guests' passports or national ID cards prior to or during check-in. You then log into the secure Alloggiati Web portal using your assigned digital credentials or smart card (SPID) and enter the guest's name, surname, date of birth, nationality, and document number.
- The Legal Risk: Failure to register guests is a criminal offense in Italy, not a minor administrative infraction. It can lead to criminal prosecution, a notation on your police record, arrest for up to three months, or personal fines up to €206 per unregistered guest.

### Tourist Tax Collection and Remittance

Almost every major tourist destination in Italy charges a tourist tax (imposta di soggiorno). The tax is paid by the guest per night, but the property owner is legally responsible for collecting and remitting it.

- Rates: Rates are set independently by each municipality and often scale based on the property's classification or location. For example, smaller towns in Puglia or Sicily may charge €1.50 to €3.00 per night. Major cities charge significantly more. In Florence, the rate is €5.50, and in Milan, due to Olympic funding and municipal increases, short-term rental tourist taxes can reach €9.50 per person per night.
- Exemptions: Most cities exempt children under a certain age (typically under 10 or 12 years old), disabled guests, their companions, and residents of that specific municipality.
- Remittance: You must compile the collected taxes and submit a formal declaration to the municipality, usually on a quarterly basis. Payment is made digitally through the pagoPA system.

## What Are the Fines for Non-Compliant Holiday Let Operations?

Operating a holiday let in Italy without proper licensing triggers heavy administrative penalties, including fines up to €8,000 for missing CIN codes and €10,000 for operating without a SCIA. Additionally, advertising a listing without showing the mandatory identification code can result in immediate platform delisting and fines up to €5,000.

### The Cost of Cutting Corners

The Italian government has empowered the Guardia di Finanza (Finance Police), local municipal police forces, and tax inspectors to conduct random audits of online listings and physical properties. They cross-reference active listings on Airbnb and Booking.com with the BDSR database. If they discover an active rental without a registered CIN, or with a missing safety device, they will issue immediate fines.

| Infraction | Legal Basis / Framework | Administrative Fine Range | Additional Penalties |
|---|---|---|---|
| Missing National CIN Code | Article 13-ter, DL 145/2023 | €800 to €8,000 | Immediate listing deactivation on platforms. |
| Failure to Display CIN Code | Article 13-ter, DL 145/2023 | €500 to €5,000 | Mandatory deletion of promotional listings. |
| Missing Safety Equipment | Article 13-ter, DL 145/2023 | €600 to €6,000 | Potential suspension of rental activities. |
| No SCIA (For 3 or More Units) | Municipal SUAP Regulations | €2,000 to €10,000 | Complete business closure and asset seals. |
| Unregistered Guests | Article 109, TULPS (Criminal) | Up to €206 per guest | Criminal prosecution; potential prison up to 3 months. |
| Tourist Tax Non-Remittance | Municipal Tax Ordinances | 30% of unpaid tax amount | Additional interest charges and tax audit trigger. |

## How Does DAC7 and Tax Reporting Work for Italian Holiday Lets?

Under the EU DAC7 directive, online booking platforms automatically report short-term rental transaction data and tax identification details directly to the Italian Revenue Agency (Agenzia delle Entrate) every year. Private hosts pay a flat-rate tax of 21% on their first property and 26% on their second, with no business registration required.

### DAC7: The End of Unreported Rental Income

The EU DAC7 Directive (Directive 2021/514) has transformed tax enforcement for holiday lets across Europe. Under this rule, online booking platforms are legally required to collect, verify, and report financial and personal data of all hosts to European tax authorities annually.

In Italy, this means the Agenzia delle Entrate automatically receives a detailed report of your gross rental income, the number of nights rented, your bank account details, and your property’s address. It is practically impossible to hide rental income from the tax authorities if you use major online booking channels.

### The Cedolare Secca Tax Regime

If you operate your holiday let as a non-professional individual (managing one or two properties), you can opt for the highly advantageous flat-rate tax regime known as Cedolare Secca.

- First Property: Taxed at a flat rate of 21% on your gross rental revenue. Under this regime, you cannot deduct any expenses (such as agency commissions, utility bills, or maintenance costs), but you avoid progressive income tax brackets (IRPEF), which can reach 43%.
- Second Property: Taxed at a flat rate of 26% on your gross rental revenue.
- Three or More Properties: You are excluded from the Cedolare Secca entirely. Your rental income is treated as business income, subject to standard VAT (IVA) and progressive IRPEF tax rates or corporate tax brackets.

### Automatic Platform Withholding

To streamline collection, platforms like Airbnb and Booking.com are required to act as withholding agents (sostituto d'imposta). They automatically withhold 21% of your gross rental revenue at source and remit it directly to the Italian Treasury. 

At the end of the fiscal year, the platform issues a Certificazione Unica (CU) document showing the exact amount withheld. You must provide this document to your Italian accountant when filing your annual tax return (Dichiarazione dei Redditi) to reconcile your final tax liability. If you owe 26% on a second property, you will pay the remaining 5% during your annual tax filing.

Many foreign investors spend months finding a perfect apartment in Florence or Milan, secure their tax code, and apply for a CIN, only to discover that the building’s condominium regulations explicitly ban short-term holiday rentals. In Italy, a unanimous or majority-approved condominium regulation is legally binding.
