# IVIE and IVAFE: Italy Wealth Tax on Foreign Assets for Property Owners

Quick answer: IVIE and IVAFE are Italian wealth taxes that apply only when you are tax resident in Italy and hold assets outside Italy. A British, American, or German buyer who owns an Italian holiday home but remains tax resident abroad pays IMU on that Italian property and does not pay IVIE on it. IVIE targets foreign real estate owned by Italian residents. IVAFE targets foreign bank and investment accounts at 0.2% per year. If you relocate to Italy and keep a home abroad, both taxes can apply alongside IMU on any Italian second home. The [Article 24-bis flat tax regime](/guides/italy-flat-tax-regime-new-residents/) exempts qualifying new residents from IVIE and IVAFE entirely.

## What are IVIE and IVAFE in the Italian tax system?

IVIE (*Imposta sul valore degli immobili situati all'estero*) is a national wealth tax on the value of real estate located outside Italy owned by Italian tax residents. IVAFE (*Imposta sul valore delle attività finanziarie detenute all'estero*) is a parallel levy on foreign financial assets, including bank balances, securities, and certain insurance products. Both were introduced in 2014 to align Italy with OECD transparency standards and to tax worldwide asset bases of residents who previously reported only Italian-sourced income.

Neither tax applies to property or accounts inside Italy. Domestic real estate falls under IMU and related local levies. Domestic financial income is taxed through IRPEF, capital gains rules, or the 26% flat rate on savings where applicable. IVIE and IVAFE exist specifically to capture offshore wealth held by people whom Italy treats as fiscal residents.

For international buyers researching [how to buy property in Italy as a foreigner](/guides/buy-property-italy-foreigner/), the distinction matters at purchase and at relocation. Buying Italian bricks does not create IVIE exposure by itself. Becoming Italian tax resident while keeping a London flat, a Swiss chalet, or a Miami condo does.

## Who counts as an Italian tax resident versus a non-resident?

Italian tax residency follows Article 2 of the TUIR. You are generally tax resident if any one of three tests is met for the greater part of the calendar year: you are registered in the municipal population registry (*anagrafe*), your center of vital interests (family and economic ties) is in Italy, or you are physically present in Italy for more than 183 days (184 in a leap year).

Non-residents are individuals who fail all three tests for that year. A German executive who spends six weeks annually in a Puglia trullo but lives and works in Munich remains a non-resident. A Milan-based professional registered at the Comune, with children in local schools and an Italian employment contract, is almost certainly resident even if they also spend summers abroad.

Residency is assessed year by year. Moving mid-year can split obligations: days before relocation may leave you non-resident; days after may trigger resident rules including IVIE on foreign property held at 31 December. Visa status alone does not determine tax residency. An Elective Residency Visa holder who exceeds 183 days in Italy typically becomes tax resident and enters the IVIE/IVAFE perimeter unless another regime applies.

| Status indicator | Typical non-resident profile | Typical Italian tax resident profile |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Municipal registration | No *residenza* in Italy | Registered at Italian *anagrafe* |
| Physical presence | Under 183 days in Italy per year | Over 183 days, or habitual abode in Italy |
| Economic center | Employment, business, main bank abroad | Italian salary, business, or primary lifestyle in Italy |
| Italian property tax on Italy asset | IMU on second homes; possible cedolare secca on rent | IMU if second home; prima casa exemption if main home |
| Tax on foreign property | None in Italy (home country rules apply) | IVIE at 0.76% (or reduced rate if main home abroad) |
| Tax on foreign bank accounts | None in Italy | IVAFE at 0.2% (0.4% in blacklisted jurisdictions) |

This table is the anchor for every scenario below. If you are unsure which column applies, treat residency as a professional determination before you buy, let, or relocate.

## Which tax applies to Italian property versus foreign property?

The location of the asset and your residency status together determine the tax. Italy does not stack IVIE on top of IMU for the same Italian villa. IVIE never applies to Italian situs real estate. IMU never applies to a Paris apartment owned by an Italian resident; IVIE does.

### Table 2: Property location vs taxpayer residency

| Your tax status | Property in Italy | Property outside Italy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Non-resident foreign owner | IMU (annual municipal tax); Italian rent taxed via cedolare secca or IRPEF; no IVIE | No Italian tax on the foreign asset; report in home country |
| Italian tax resident | IMU if second home or luxury primary; exempt if ordinary prima casa | IVIE 0.76% on value; Quadro RW disclosure |
| Flat tax resident (Art. 24-bis) | IMU still due on Italian property; Italian rental income outside lump sum | IVIE and IVAFE exempt; Quadro RW exempt on foreign assets |
| Italian citizen living abroad (non-resident) | IMU on Italian holiday home | No IVIE (not Italian tax resident) |

Foreign nationals are not singled out. A US citizen tax resident in Rome faces the same IVIE on a Colorado cabin as an Italian citizen tax resident in Rome. Conversely, an Italian citizen who moved to Dubai, ceased Italian tax residency, and kept a Liguria apartment pays IMU on Liguria only, not IVIE on UAE assets.

Critical clarification for Italian Estate readers: If you are American, British, or Australian and you only own property in Italy while remaining tax resident at home, Italy charges you IMU on that Italian asset. You do not file IVIE on your Italian apartment. IVIE would apply only if you later become Italian tax resident and still own property in your home country or elsewhere outside Italy.

## How does IVIE work on foreign real estate?

IVIE is calculated at 0.76% per year on the taxable value of each foreign property. A minimum tax of €200 per property applies when IVIE is due. A reduced 0.4% rate can apply when the foreign property qualifies as your main dwelling abroad and you do not own a primary residence in Italy, under strict factual tests. Most Italian residents who buy a primary home in Italy and keep a former main home abroad as a second property pay the standard 0.76% on that foreign unit.

### Valuation rules

Valuation drives the bill. Italy uses a hierarchy:

1. EU/EEA cadastral or equivalent values where the foreign state publishes them (common for French *valeur locative*, Spanish cadastral references, etc.).
2. Purchase price for the first five calendar years of ownership when no cadastral value exists.
3. Revalued cost after five years using statutory coefficients published by the Ministry of Economy and Finance, updated periodically.

Renovation costs capitalized in the foreign jurisdiction may increase basis where documented. Market appraisals alone do not replace the statutory hierarchy unless Italian rules specifically allow them for that country.

### Payment and filing

IVIE is declared in Quadro RM of the Modello Redditi PF (personal income tax return) for the year in which you were resident on 31 December. Payment is due with the income tax balance, typically by 30 June of the following year, with installment options mirroring IRPEF. Properties acquired or sold mid-year are pro-rated by months of ownership.

You must also complete Quadro RW (*monitoring* of foreign assets) unless exempt. RW does not itself generate tax but failure to file triggers penalties from €258 upward per omitted asset, independent of IVIE due.

### Example: Italian resident with UK holiday home and Tuscan second home

Marco relocates from London to Florence in 2025, registers *residenza*, and keeps his Chelsea flat (market value £900,000) plus a small Chianti house. On 31 December 2025 he is Italian tax resident.

- Chianti house: IMU as second home (or prima casa if he registers it as main residence and qualifies).
- Chelsea flat: IVIE at 0.76% on UK valuation per HMRC/cadastral rules accepted by Italy, minimum €200.
- UK bank account: IVAFE at 0.2% on average balance.

If Marco had not moved to Italy and remained UK tax resident, he would pay IMU only on Chianti, zero IVIE on Chelsea in Italy (HMRC would tax worldwide income under UK rules instead).

## How does IVAFE work on foreign financial assets?

IVAFE applies at 0.2% per year on the average value of financial assets held abroad during the tax year: bank accounts, brokerage portfolios, bonds, mutual funds, and certain life policies with investment wrappers. Accounts in jurisdictions on Italy's blacklist (*paradisi fiscali*) face 0.4%. A minimum IVAFE of €50 applies when tax is owed.

IVAFE is independent of whether foreign investments generated dividends or interest. Even a non-income-producing custody account can trigger IVAFE if balances are positive on average. Italian residents with foreign pension pots or company stock plans need specialist mapping; some wrappers are excluded, others are not.

Reporting sits in the same Quadro RM as IVIE. Quadro RW lists each foreign account with IBAN, institution, and peak values. Flat tax residents under Article 24-bis skip both IVAFE payment and RW disclosure on foreign financial assets for the regime duration.

| Asset type | Typical IVAFE treatment | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Foreign current and savings accounts | 0.2% on average balance | All currencies converted at year-end Banca d'Italia rate |
| Foreign brokerage / ETF portfolios | 0.2% on average market value | Includes US and UK ISAs if treated as foreign financial assets |
| Foreign pension (non-Italian) | Case-by-case | Some EU pensions excluded; US 401(k) often reportable |
| Crypto on foreign exchanges | Often reportable in RW | IVIE/IVAFE treatment evolving; commercialista review required |
| Italian bank accounts | Not IVAFE | Taxed under domestic rules instead |

## What taxes do non-residents pay on Italian property instead of IVIE?

Non-resident owners of Italian real estate pay IMU, Italy's annual municipal property tax, on second homes and on luxury primary residences. They do not pay IVIE on that Italian asset because IVIE applies only to foreign situs property.

Non-residents also face:

- Registration tax at purchase (9% on second homes using *prezzo-valore*, or 2% for qualifying prima casa if they establish residency).
- Cedolare secca at 21% or 26% on rental income if elected, or IRPEF on Italian-source rent.
- Plusvalenza on gains if selling within five years, per [Italy capital gains tax on property](/guides/italy-capital-gains-tax-property/).
- Withholding on certain Italian-source income.

None of these is a substitute for IVIE. They are the correct Italian taxes for non-residents holding Italian bricks. Budget IMU before underwriting yield using the dedicated [IMU property tax guide](/guides/imu-property-tax-italy/).

A French couple who buy in Sicily and remain tax resident in Paris file IMU and Italian rental taxes locally. They ignore IVIE in Italy entirely. Their French tax return may include worldwide property under French rules, but that is a French obligation, not IVIE.

## What happens when you relocate to Italy or leave Italy?

Inbound relocation is the main IVIE trigger for property investors. If you spend years as a non-resident owner of a Rome apartment, then move to Italy, register residency, and keep a house in your origin country, IVIE starts on the foreign house from the year you become resident. IMU continues on the Rome unit unless it becomes your registered prima casa.

Outbound relocation reverses the pattern. If you leave Italy, deregister from the *anagrafe*, and re-establish tax residency abroad, IVIE and IVAFE generally cease for the year you become non-resident, assessed on a pro-rata basis if you split the year. IMU on Italian property you retain still applies because IMU follows property location, not owner residency.

Investors comparing [Prima Casa vs second home tax treatment](/guides/italy-prima-casa-vs-second-home-tax/) should model IMU savings on an Italian primary residence separately from IVIE on any foreign property they refuse to sell before moving.

## How does the flat tax regime interact with IVIE and IVAFE?

Article 24-bis flat tax residency offers a fixed annual lump sum (€200,000 for new entrants after August 2024, €100,000 for grandfathered relocations) on foreign-sourced income, plus full exemption from IVIE, IVAFE, and Quadro RW on foreign assets. See the full [Italy flat tax regime guide](/guides/italy-flat-tax-regime-new-residents/) for eligibility (9 of 10 prior years non-resident) and family extensions.

Important nuance: flat tax covers foreign income, not Italian-source income. Rental income from an Italian apartment remains taxable in Italy. IMU on Italian property is still due. IVIE exemption does not eliminate IMU on a Milan penthouse or a Puglia masseria.

For high-net-worth buyers planning relocation, the IVIE/IVAFE exemption alone can save tens of thousands annually on a global portfolio, often more than the flat tax cost once foreign financial assets exceed roughly €10 million (0.2% IVAFE = €20,000) plus foreign real estate at €5 million (0.76% IVIE ≈ €38,000).

### Table 3: Standard resident vs flat tax resident on same asset mix

| Asset / obligation | Standard Italian tax resident | Article 24-bis flat tax resident |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Foreign portfolio €8m | IVAFE ≈ €16,000/year + RW filing | €0 IVAFE; no RW on foreign assets |
| Foreign holiday home €2m | IVIE ≈ €15,200/year (0.76%) + RW | €0 IVIE |
| Italian second home | IMU per municipal rate | IMU still due |
| Italian rental income | IRPEF or cedolare secca | Taxed separately, not in lump sum |
| Foreign dividends | IRPEF or 26% + IVAFE on account | Covered by lump sum; no IVAFE |
| Annual compliance | Modello Redditi + RW + RM | Lump sum payment; simplified foreign reporting |

## How do IVIE and IVAFE interact with double tax treaties?

Italy has tax treaties with many countries. IVIE is designed as a wealth tax on foreign real estate, not income tax. Treaty relief for IVIE is limited compared with IRPEF credits. Some treaties allow credit for similar foreign property taxes paid abroad against IVIE, capped at the Italian tax due. IVAFE rarely has a direct foreign counterpart; Swiss wealth taxes or Spanish *Impuesto sobre el Patrimonio* may interact depending on facts.

Treaty credit mechanics are technical. A UK resident who becomes Italian tax resident might pay UK council tax and Italian IVIE on the same English house in overlapping ways; treaty analysis and domestic UK rules determine net cost. Always coordinate an Italian commercialista with a home-country adviser before relocating.

## What are common planning mistakes property owners make?

Assuming IMU and IVIE are interchangeable. They are not. IMU is municipal tax on Italian property. IVIE is national tax on foreign property for residents. Foreign owners who never relocate never touch IVIE.

Buying Italian property and thinking IVIE applies. It does not. Non-residents pay IMU. Full stop on the IVIE question for the Italian asset.

Failing to deregister before selling a foreign home. IVIE is due for months of ownership in the year of sale. Keep purchase and sale deeds for basis and pro-rata calculations.

Ignoring RW when IVIE is zero. Some foreign assets trigger RW monitoring even when IVIE is nil due to valuation quirks. Missing RW carries penalties.

Relocating without modelling IMU + IVIE together. A buyer who registers prima casa in Rome but keeps a €3 million Swiss chalet adds roughly €22,800 IVIE annually at 0.76% on top of any IMU on Italian second holdings.

Electing flat tax without confirming Italian rental strategy. Flat tax exempts foreign wealth taxes but not Italian rental tax or IMU. Model Italian yield net of cedolare secca separately.

## How should you file and pay IVIE and IVAFE in 2026?

1. Confirm tax residency status with your commercialista before 31 December.
2. Gather foreign property values (cadastral extracts or purchase deeds) and average financial balances.
3. Complete Quadro RW for each foreign asset unless flat tax exempt.
4. Calculate Quadro RM for IVIE and IVAFE amounts.
5. Submit Modello Redditi PF and pay via F24 by 30 June 2027 for tax year 2026 (or use authorized installments).

Non-residents with only Italian property typically file Modello 720 or simplified regimes for Italian-source income only, not IVIE/IVAFE. Non-resident IMU remains a separate F24 payment in June and December per the [IMU guide](/guides/imu-property-tax-italy/).
