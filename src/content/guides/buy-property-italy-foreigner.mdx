# Buy Property in Italy as a Foreigner: Complete 2026 Guide

Quick Answer: Foreigners can legally buy property in Italy. EU citizens face no restrictions, while non-EU citizens must either have reciprocity with Italy or hold Italian residency. The process involves obtaining a codice fiscale, signing a preliminary contract (compromesso), and completing the final deed (rogito) before a notaio. Budget 10-15% of purchase price for closing costs. ## Who Can Buy Property in Italy?

The ability to purchase Italian real estate depends on your citizenship and residency status. Italy operates under a principle of reciprocity for non-EU nationals, while EU citizens enjoy unrestricted access.

### EU, EEA, and Swiss Citizens

Citizens of European Union member states, European Economic Area countries, and Switzerland can purchase any type of Italian real estate under identical conditions to Italian nationals. No reciprocity verification, residence requirements, or additional documentation is needed beyond the standard purchase process.

### Non-EU Citizens: Reciprocity Rule

Non-EU nationals must satisfy Italy's Principle of Reciprocity as outlined in Article 16 of the Preliminary Provisions to the Italian Civil Code. This means you can acquire Italian property only if your home country grants similar rights to Italian citizens.

| Country Status | Can Buy Property | Restrictions |
|---|---|---|
| EU/EEA/Swiss | ✅ Yes, unrestricted | None |
| US Citizens | ✅ Yes, full reciprocity | None |
| UK Citizens | ✅ Yes, full reciprocity | None |
| Australian Citizens | ✅ Yes, full reciprocity | None |
| Canadian Citizens | ⚠️ Restricted | Limited non-resident purchases |
| Other Countries | 🔍 Check MAECI tables | Varies by country |

The Italian Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MAECI) maintains official reciprocity tables on esteri.it, which notaries consult before signing any deed. These tables are updated periodically and serve as the definitive source for reciprocity status.

### Alternative: Italian Residence Permit

If your country lacks reciprocity, you can still purchase Italian property by obtaining a valid Italian residence permit. This grants you the same acquisition rights as an Italian resident, regardless of your home country's reciprocity status.

### Codice Fiscale (Tax Identification Number)

Every foreign buyer must obtain an Italian codice fiscale, a 16-character alphanumeric tax identification number. This code is mandatory for all property transactions and Italian administrative dealings.

How to obtain:
- From abroad: Visit any Italian consulate with passport and completed application
- In Italy: Visit Agenzia delle Entrate offices with valid ID
- Processing time: Usually same-day issuance
- Cost: Free of charge

### Banking Arrangements

Open an Italian bank account to facilitate the transaction, though this isn't always mandatory. Italian banks typically require:
- Valid passport and codice fiscale
- Proof of income or employment
- Initial deposit (varies by bank)
- Utility bill or rental agreement for address verification

### Step 1: Property Search and Initial Offer

Once you've identified a property, submit a formal written offer called a proposta d'acquisto. This document includes:
- Purchase price and payment terms
- Deposit amount (typically €5,000-€10,000)
- Acceptance deadline for the seller
- Any conditions or contingencies

The seller has a specified timeframe to accept, reject, or counter your offer. If accepted, both parties are legally bound to proceed with the transaction.

### Step 2: Preliminary Contract (Compromesso)

The compromesso or contratto preliminare is a binding preliminary agreement that commits both parties to the future sale. Key elements include:

- Deposit payment: Typically 10-20% of purchase price
- Completion deadline: Usually 30-60 days from signing
- Property description: Detailed specifications and boundaries
- Conditions precedent: Mortgage approval, permits, etc.

| Deposit Amount | Property Type | Typical Range |
|---|---|---|
| Urban apartments | €20,000-€100,000+ | 10-15% of price |
| Rural properties | €50,000-€200,000+ | 15-20% of price |
| Luxury properties | €100,000+ | 10-20% of price |

Legal implications: The compromesso creates a binding obligation to complete the purchase. If the buyer withdraws, they forfeit the deposit. If the seller withdraws, they must pay double the deposit amount to the buyer.

### Step 3: Due Diligence Period

Between signing the compromesso and the final deed, conduct thorough due diligence. This critical period allows you to verify the property's legal status and identify potential issues.

#### Technical Survey (Geometra)

Hire a licensed geometra (chartered surveyor) to conduct a comprehensive technical assessment:

Services provided:
- Cadastral compliance check: Verify property matches official records (visura catastale)
- Urban planning compliance: Ensure all modifications have proper permits
- Boundary verification: Confirm property limits match cadastral maps
- Building permit audit: Check all construction was properly authorized
- Structural assessment: Identify any structural issues or defects

Cost: €500-€1,500 depending on property size and complexity

#### Legal Due Diligence

Your lawyer should perform:
- Title verification: Confirm seller's legal ownership
- Mortgage search: Check for outstanding liens or encumbrances
- Planning compliance: Verify all permits and authorizations
- Condominium review: For apartments, examine management records
- Tax verification: Ensure all property taxes are current

## The Notaio: Your Transaction Guardian

The notaio is a government-appointed public official who serves as the cornerstone of Italian property transactions. Unlike common law systems, the notaio acts for both parties and the state, with personal liability for transaction validity.

### Notaio Responsibilities

1. Title verification: Confirm seller's legal ownership and authority to sell
2. Reciprocity check: Verify non-EU buyers meet reciprocity requirements  
3. Mortgage clearance: Ensure all liens and encumbrances are resolved
4. Tax calculation: Determine correct taxes and collect payments
5. Document preparation: Draft the final deed (rogito or atto di compravendita)
6. Registration: File the transfer with land registry and tax authorities

### Visura Catastale and Cadastral Compliance

The notaio performs mandatory searches at the Land Registry (Catasto) through a visura catastale to verify:
- Property's fiscal identity and boundaries
- Current ownership registration  
- Cadastral category and value
- Any registered mortgages or liens

Critical requirement: Since July 2010, Italian law mandates that the seller declare the property's actual state matches the cadastral floor plan (planimetria). If discrepancies exist, the deed is void unless corrected through a DOCFA variation filed by a geometra.

### Step 4: Final Deed (Rogito)

The rogito or atto di compravendita is the final notarial deed that transfers ownership. This ceremony typically occurs at the notary's office with both parties present.

What happens at the rogito:
- Final payment transfer (usually via banker's draft)
- Document signing by all parties
- Tax payment and receipt
- Immediate property registration
- Key handover

Required at closing:
- Balance of purchase price
- All closing costs and taxes
- Valid identification documents
- Codice fiscale confirmation
- Power of attorney (if buying remotely)

### Power of Attorney Requirements

The POA must be:
- Notarized: Executed before a notary in your home country
- Apostilled: Authenticated under the 1961 Hague Convention
- Translated: Into Italian by a certified translator
- Specific: Clearly define the property and transaction terms
- Limited in time: Include expiration date and scope limitations

Typical POA costs: €300-€800 for preparation, notarization, and apostille

Your representative can attend the compromesso signing, coordinate with professionals, and complete the final rogito on your behalf. However, ensure you trust your representative completely, as they wield significant authority over a major financial transaction.

## Due Diligence Deep Dive

Italian property risk clusters around cadastral mismatches, unauthorized layout changes, pending condominium extraordinary works, and STR licensing gaps that agents omit from English summaries. Independent avvocato and geometra review before compromesso beats post-deposit discovery of conformità blocks, CIN delisting risk, or spese spikes within the first ownership year.


### Cadastral Compliance (Conformità Catastale)

The **visura catastale** provides the property's official fiscal identity, but doesn't guarantee legal ownership. Key checks include:

Planimetria verification: The registered floor plan must match the property's actual layout. Any discrepancies (added rooms, modified spaces, enclosed balconies) must be corrected via a DOCFA variation before the rogito.

Boundary accuracy: Ensure property boundaries match the cadastral map (mappa catastale), particularly important for rural properties or houses with gardens.

Ownership registration: Verify the seller appears as the registered owner and has authority to sell.

### Urban Planning Compliance (Conformità Urbanistica)

Separate from cadastral records, urban planning compliance ensures the building was constructed and modified according to municipal permits.

Required permits verification: - **Original building permit** (licenza edilizia or permesso di costruire)
- **Modification permits** (CILA, SCIA, or building permits for changes)
- **Certificate of habitability** (certificato di abitabilità or agibilità)

Common compliance issues: - Enclosed balconies without permits
- Added rooms or bathrooms
- Modified building footprint
- Illegal pool installations
- Unauthorized garage conversions

### Condominium Due Diligence

For apartment purchases, examine the condominium's financial and legal health:

Management review: - Administrator appointment and credentials
- Annual budgets and financial statements  
- Outstanding maintenance assessments
- Pending litigation or disputes
- Building insurance coverage

Technical assessment: - Structural condition reports
- Major maintenance schedules
- Energy efficiency certificates
- Shared facility conditions

### High-Risk Property Types

Inherited properties: Often have complex ownership chains and potential undisclosed heirs. Require extended title searches and family tree verification.

Properties with planning violations: Buildings with unauthorized modifications face potential demolition orders or massive regularization costs.

Rural properties near protected areas: May have severe building restrictions or environmental compliance requirements.

Properties in seismic zones: Require specialized structural assessments and compliance with anti-seismic regulations.

### Warning Signs to Avoid

- Seller pressure for rapid completion without due diligence
- Properties priced significantly below market value
- Incomplete or missing documentation
- Recent ownership transfers without clear provenance
- Properties with multiple previous sale attempts
- Unusual payment structures or requests for cash

### United States Citizens

Full reciprocity exists between the US and Italy. American citizens can purchase any type of Italian property without restrictions. The notaio confirms reciprocity through MAECI tables before closing.

### United Kingdom Citizens

Despite Brexit, UK citizens maintain full reciprocity rights for Italian property purchases. No additional restrictions or requirements apply beyond standard non-EU procedures.

### Canadian Citizens

Canada has **limited reciprocity** with Italy. While Canadian residents can generally purchase Italian property, some provinces restrict foreign property ownership, which may affect reciprocity status. The notaio will verify current reciprocity status through official MAECI channels.

### Australian Citizens

Full reciprocity exists between Australia and Italy. Australian citizens face no restrictions on Italian property purchases.

### Other Countries

For countries not listed, check the current MAECI reciprocity tables at esteri.it. Reciprocity status can change, so verify with the notaio during the preliminary contract phase.

### Property Taxes

IMU (Municipal Property Tax): Applies to second homes and luxury properties. Primary residences (prima casa) are generally exempt. Rates vary by municipality from 0.4% to 1.06% of cadastral value.

TARI (Waste Tax): Annual waste collection tax ranging from €100-€500 depending on property size and location.

### Ongoing Compliance

Residence registration: Prima casa buyers must establish municipal residency within 18 months or face tax penalty recovery.

Utility setup: Register for electricity, gas, water, and internet services using your codice fiscale.

Property insurance: While not mandatory, comprehensive property insurance is highly recommended.

### Procedural Errors

- **Skipping technical surveys:** Costly compliance issues discovered after purchase
- **Inadequate legal representation:** Relying solely on the notaio who represents both parties
- **Rushed due diligence:** Missing critical title or compliance issues
- **Currency timing:** Not hedging exchange rate risk for large transactions

### Documentation Failures

- **Invalid power of attorney:** Incorrect notarization or apostille procedures
- **Missing translations:** Delays due to improperly certified document translations
- **Codice fiscale delays:** Starting the process without obtaining tax identification first

### Financial Miscalculations

- **Underestimating closing costs:** Budget shortfalls at completion
- **Ignoring ongoing costs:** Unexpected property taxes and maintenance fees
- **Poor currency planning:** Exchange rate fluctuations affecting purchase budget
