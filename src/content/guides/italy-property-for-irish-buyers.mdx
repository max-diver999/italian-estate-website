## Why do Irish buyers have a legal advantage over UK purchasers in 2026?

Irish nationals hold EU citizenship, which removes the main friction that British buyers manage after Brexit. Under Article 16 of the Italian Civil Code preliminary provisions, non-EU purchasers must satisfy reciprocity between Italy and their home country. Irish buyers skip that entirely. Your notaio does not consult MAECI reciprocity tables. You sign the compromesso and rogito on the same legal footing as an Italian resident buyer.

The contrast with UK buyers is operational, not theoretical. British nationals remain eligible to buy Italian property because Italy preserved full reciprocity with the United Kingdom, but every UK purchase requires notaio verification against MAECI databases before rogito. Irish purchases do not. UK owners also face the Schengen 90/180-day tourist stay limit unless they hold a national visa. Irish EU citizens rely on EU free movement law for longer stays when they register locally, a separate track from property title.

Ireland's non-Schengen status confuses some first-time buyers. You do not use the Schengen visa waiver at the border because Ireland is outside the Schengen Area. You still exercise EU citizen rights in Italy under Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union rules. That means no reciprocity gate on purchase and a clearer path to anagrafe registration if you plan extended living, compared with the UK third-country pathway documented in our [UK buyer guide](/guides/italy-property-for-uk-buyers/).

## How does shared euro pricing change the Irish buyer budget?

Irish buyers fund Italian property in the same currency they earn primary salary in, a structural advantage over UK purchasers managing sterling-euro volatility on every compromesso deposit and rogito balance. When Ireland and Italy both price in euro, your budget line items align without forward FX contracts on each tranche.

That does not eliminate all financial planning work. Irish buyers who hold investment portfolios in USD-listed assets or receive sterling rental income from UK property still convert those flows before Italian payments. The purchase itself, Italian mortgage debt, IMU, condominium spese, and cedolare secca settlements run in euro natively.

## How do Irish buyers obtain a codice fiscale in Dublin?

Every Irish property buyer needs an Italian codice fiscale, a sixteen-character tax identification number. Banks, the notaio, utility companies, and Agenzia delle Entrate all require it. You cannot sign compromesso or register rogito without one.

Irish residents typically apply through the Embassy of Italy in Ireland at 63-65 Northumberland Road, Dublin 4. Submit form AA4/8 with passport copy and proof of Irish home address. Processing ranges from same-day at busy periods to two weeks by appointment. The code itself is free.

If you are already in Italy for viewings, visit any Agenzia delle Entrate office with passport and completed form. Counter issuance is usually immediate. Remote buyers often grant power of attorney to an Italian lawyer to secure the code before flying back.

Apply before you make a binding offer, not after. Spring inspection queues spike when Irish buyers tour Tuscany and Puglia after Easter school breaks. A missing tax ID delays mortgage underwriting by two to four weeks because Italian banks cannot open loan files without it. Full application paths and proxy options sit in [codice fiscale for Italy property](/guides/codice-fiscale-italy-property/).

## How does the Italian purchase process work for Irish EU citizens?

The transaction sequence is identical to Italian domestic buyers. No additional ministry approvals apply to Irish nationals.

**Step 1, Codice fiscale:** Obtain your Italian tax identification number before any offer or mortgage application. Apply at the Italian embassy in Dublin, or in person at Agenzia delle Entrate if you are already in Italy.

**Step 2, Offer and compromesso:** Submit a written proposta d'acquisto, then sign the preliminary contract (compromesso) with a 10-20% deposit. Insert suspensive conditions for mortgage approval and satisfactory due diligence. Our [compromesso guide](/guides/compromesso-italy-property-contract/) explains deposit forfeiture rules and transcription options.

**Step 3, Due diligence:** Engage a geometra for cadastral compliance, an independent lawyer (not the seller's agent), and verify IMU, condominium debts, and building permits. Irish buyers should not skip structural surveys on pre-1980 rural stock. Follow the checklist in [due diligence for Italian property](/guides/due-diligence-italy-property/).

**Step 4, Rogito:** The notaio executes the final deed, collects registration tax, registers ownership, and records any mortgage ipoteca. Timeline from compromesso to rogito typically runs 60-90 days for cash buyers, 90-120 days with financing.

**Step 5, Post-closing compliance:** Register utilities, pay IMU annually on second homes, obtain CIN before listing for short-term rental, and appoint a commercialista for Italian tax filings on rental income.

Irish buyers often grant a notarized power of attorney (procura speciale) to a trusted representative in Ireland or Italy to sign documents when they cannot attend rogito in person. The POA must be notarized in Ireland and apostilled under Hague Convention rules.

## What tax rules should Irish buyers understand before purchasing?

Tax planning crosses two jurisdictions. Italy taxes the property and income generated there. Ireland taxes worldwide income and gains for Irish-resident individuals. This section is informational, not personal tax advice. Engage an Irish tax adviser with Italy experience before signing.

### Purchase taxes: second home default for most Irish buyers

Most Irish buyers acquire second homes (seconda casa) because they do not register Italian residency within 18 months. That triggers 9% registration tax on cadastral value for private-seller purchases, plus notary and agency costs. Prima casa at 2% registration tax requires anagrafe residency and is rarely compatible with keeping tax residence in Ireland.

Compare regimes in our [Italy prima casa vs second home tax](/guides/italy-prima-casa-vs-second-home-tax/) guide.

### Annual ownership: IMU and local levies

Second homes pay IMU municipal property tax annually, typically 0.4-0.76% of cadastral value depending on comune and property class. TARI waste tax and condominium spese add €2,000-€8,000 yearly on coastal apartments. IMU is deductible if you report rental income under ordinary IRPEF rather than cedolare secca. See [IMU property tax Italy](/guides/imu-property-tax-italy/) for cadastral versus market price interactions.

### Rental income taxation in Italy

Long-term residential leases: cedolare secca flat tax at 21% for residents, with non-resident landlords often at 26% via IRPEF unless electing cedolare where permitted. Short-term rentals: 26% cedolare secca for non-residents on affitti brevi income. Full STR compliance in [short-term rental rules Italy](/guides/short-term-rental-rules-italy/).

### Irish CGT and income tax overlay

Irish tax residents remain liable for Irish tax on worldwide income and chargeable gains. Key lines Irish buyers model:

- **Irish rental income:** Foreign rental receipts from Italian property generally enter Irish income tax computation under Schedule D rules, with credit for Italian tax paid under the Ireland-Italy double taxation convention.
- **Irish CGT on disposal:** Investment and holiday properties typically fall outside principal private residence relief. Irish CGT at 33% may apply on chargeable gains when you sell, subject to allowable acquisition and improvement costs and any treaty relief for Italian tax already charged on the same gain.
- **No Irish wealth tax on property:** Unlike Dutch box 3 deemed-return regimes, Ireland does not impose a parallel annual wealth charge on foreign real estate solely for ownership. IMU remains the primary recurring Italian holding cost.

Always reconcile Italian cedolare elections with Irish reporting. Electing 26% flat tax in Italy does not automatically eliminate Irish income tax filing obligations.

| Tax item | Italy (second home, non-resident) | Ireland (tax resident) | Treaty note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Purchase registration tax | 9% on cadastral value | Not applicable | N/A |
| Annual IMU | 0.4-0.76% cadastral value | Not directly taxed | N/A |
| Long-term rental income | 21-26% cedolare/IRPEF | Income tax at marginal rates | Foreign tax credit typical |
| Short-term rental income | 26% cedolare secca | Income tax at marginal rates | Foreign tax credit typical |
| Capital gains on sale | 26% IRPEF if sold within 5 years | 33% CGT on chargeable gain | Credit mechanism applies |
| Wealth tax on property | None (IMU only) | No annual wealth tax on foreign RE | N/A |

## Can Irish buyers obtain mortgage financing for Italian property?

Yes. Irish non-residents access Italian mutuo ipotecario products and, alternatively, Ireland-secured financing against existing assets.

### Italian bank mortgages

EU citizenship gives Irish applicants a documentation advantage over post-Brexit UK files. Banks typically offer 55-60% LTV against the lower of purchase price or bank valuation, with fixed rates near 3.2-3.8% and variable Euribor-linked rates near 3.0-3.5% in mid-2026.

Active lenders for Irish buyers:

- **Intesa Sanpaolo** and **UniCredit:** International buyer desks; standard for Tuscany, Puglia, and Lazio stock.
- **BNL (BNP Paribas)** and **Banco BPM:** Competitive for EU applicants in Emilia-Romagna and northern coastal markets.
- **Irish retail banks (AIB, Bank of Ireland, Permanent TSB):** Seldom lend directly against Italian collateral for retail clients; most Irish buyers use Italian mutuo or equity release on Irish property instead.

Required documents: three years Irish Revenue Form 11 or employer income verification, six to twelve months bank statements, employment contract or CRO registration for self-employed buyers, sworn Italian translations, codice fiscale, and bank-ordered perizia valuation. Allow six to ten weeks for approval plus three to four months total to rogito. Full mechanics in [non-resident mortgage Italy](/guides/non-resident-mortgage-italy/).

### Ireland-based alternatives

Some Irish buyers prefer:

- **Mortgage or equity release against Irish property** at Irish rates, then cash purchase in Italy (fastest closing, strongest negotiation position).
- **Cash purchase**, common among Irish buyers deploying pension lump sums or business exits without currency-matched income in Italy.

Model financing before offer. Italian bank valuation below agreed price reduces lendable amount and can collapse a deal if your compromesso lacks a mortgage suspensive clause.

## What rental strategies suit Irish owners of Italian second homes?

Irish buyers typically use properties four to ten weeks annually and rent the balance. Strategy choice depends on region, condominium rules, and tolerance for Italian compliance admin.

**Long-term furnished rental:** Lower gross yield (3-4.5% in Tuscany and Rome) but stable tenant base and 21% cedolare on qualifying contracts. Works in Florence periphery, Lecce urban stock, and Rome EUR districts.

**Short-term holiday let:** Higher gross potential (5-8% in permitted Puglia zones) but requires CIN registration, municipal SCIA where applicable, 26% non-resident cedolare, cleaning logistics, and seasonal vacancy outside peak months. Puglia STR peak June-September; Rome peaks spring and autumn around Jubilee tourism.

**Personal-use only:** Zero compliance overhead beyond IMU and condominium fees. Suits buyers who reject platform management and tenant access to their holiday home.

Irish owners cannot rely on Irish Residential Tenancies Board instincts for Italian leases. Italy's national CIN mandate and comune-level caps override platform defaults. Verify CIN transferability at purchase. Condominium regolamento banning tourist use kills STR plans regardless of municipal permissiveness.

## What due diligence should Irish buyers complete before compromesso?

Italian property risk clusters around cadastral mismatches, unauthorized layout changes, pending condominium extraordinary works, and STR licensing gaps that agents omit from English summaries. Independent avvocato and geometra review before compromesso beats post-deposit discovery of conformità blocks, CIN delisting risk, or spese spikes within the first ownership year.

Non-negotiable checks:

1. **Visura catastale:** room count matches physical layout; planimetria conformità or estimate cost to rectify.
2. **APE energy certificate:** Class G properties face renovation mandates; Irish buyers often walk away below Class D without price reduction.
3. **Condominium clearance:** no outstanding spese, STR permitted if planned.
4. **IMU and TARI arrears:** attach to property, not seller personally.
5. **Geometra report:** essential in Puglia rural conversions and Tuscan ruin projects.
6. **CIN and SCIA status:** for any STR-intended purchase.
7. **Mortgage suspensive clause:** if financing-dependent; specify maximum rate and LTV binding conditions.

Engage a bilingual lawyer independent of the selling agent. Closing cost line items are modelled in [cost of buying property in Italy](/guides/cost-of-buying-property-italy/).
