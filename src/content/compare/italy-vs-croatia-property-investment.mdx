## How do Adriatic prices compare between Italy and Croatia?

Adriatic price comparison in 2026 spans Italy Trieste and Friuli at 2,800 to 4,200 euro per sqm versus Croatia Split at 2,200 to 3,800 euro per sqm and Dubrovnik trophy at 4,500 to 6,500 euro per sqm before closing stacks and tax layers on each side.









Dubrovnik's UNESCO-walled core and immediate periphery trade at €3,500-5,500 per sqm for renovatable apartments and €5,000-8,000+ for turnkey sea-view units. Split's Bačvice and Varoš districts cluster at €2,800-4,200 per sqm. Istria (Rovinj, Poreč) aligns with northern Adriatic pricing at €2,500-4,500 per sqm for coastal stock, closer to Italian Friuli-Venezia Giulia than to southern Puglia.

Italy's Puglia regional average sits near €1,422 per sqm per FIAIP-tracked data, with Ostuni centro at €2,200-3,800 per sqm and emerging Carovigno corridors 15-25% below Ostuni. Polignano a Mare and Monopoli command premiums within Puglia yet still undercut Dubrovnik on a like-for-like sea-view basis. Eastern Sicily (Siracusa, Noto hinterland) offers €900-1,800 per sqm for buyers willing to trade Puglia's airport convenience for different tourism rhythm.

Transaction costs change the headline. Italy budgets 9-11% on second-home registration tax, notary, and agency fees. Croatia budgets roughly 5-8% on resale stock including 3% transfer tax, notary, and agency, though new-build VAT paths differ. A €400,000 Puglia masseria project and a €400,000 Split apartment therefore carry different all-in bases even when list prices appear similar.

## Which country offers higher rental yields on the Adriatic?

Gross yield on portal screenshots misleads Adriatic investors because both countries stack licensing, occupancy tax, and seasonal vacancy differently. Answer-first: Italy's Puglia and eastern Sicily typically deliver 5-7% gross on value stock; Croatia's coast averages 3.5-5% with Dubrovnik and Hvar at the low end.

Italy's national rental average is 4.3% per our [Italy rental yield guide](/guides/italy-rental-yield-guide/). Puglia leads Italian regions for foreign-buyer volume with 2,300 international purchases in recent FIAIP reporting and Ostuni ranking as the most-searched comune nationally. Trulli conversions and masseria pools support premium nightly rates from April through October, with shoulder weeks extending income beyond pure July-August peaks.

Croatia's yield math compresses where tourism brand outruns rent multiples. Dubrovnik old-town adjacent units achieve strong nightly rates but pay €4,000+ per sqm entry, dragging gross yield toward 3-4%. Split and Zadar improve toward 4-5.5% on apartments bought away from waterfront premiums. Istria's Rovinj market mirrors Italian Riviera seasonality with 4-5% gross on balanced purchases.

Net yield requires local tax treatment. Italy's cedolare secca offers 21% flat tax on many long-term leases and 26% on short-term tourist rentals for non-residents, plus IMU and condominium spese. Croatia collects tourist tax per guest night, applies income tax on rent through lump-sum paušal options or standard rates depending on registration, and maintains lower annual property tax than Italian IMU on equivalent stock. Budget 150-200 basis points between gross and net on both sides when modelling honestly.

## How do short-term rental rules compare in Italy and Croatia?

Both governments responded to overtourism and housing pressure by tightening short-term rental enforcement after 2022. Neither market allows informal Airbnb-style letting at scale anymore. MORE Group 2026 underwriting: model 9% second-home registration tax, 21% cedolare secca on qualifying leases, and 5-year minimum hold before compromesso deposit or visa tier wires.

Italy requires a CIN (Codice Identificativo Nazionale) on every tourist listing, comune-level SCIA or equivalent where mandated, and compliance with condominium regolamento bans in many palazzi. UNESCO municipalities including Florence, Venice, and select historic centres cap or zone STR. Puglia comuni vary: Ostuni and Carovigno actively license holiday use while rural masserie need fire and pool certificates before CIN issuance. Tax path is typically cedolare secca at 26% for STR unless long-term registration applies at 21%.

Croatia requires eVisitor registration for every guest check-in, collection of tourist tax (sojourn tax) set by municipality, and compliance with local accommodation categorisation where minpaku-style rules apply. Dubrovnik and Split introduced density and zoning constraints on unlicensed apartments. Istria harmonised registration with EU tourism statistics requirements. Platforms increasingly block unregistered listings.

Practical investor takeaway: underwrite STR only after written confirmation from the comune (Italy) or tourist board (Croatia) that your cadastral category allows nightly rentals. Cross-border buyers who assume prior owner's license transfers cleanly lose months of income on both coasts. See [Puglia property investment guide](/guides/puglia-property-investment-guide/) for CIN workflow detail on the Italian side.

## What do EU and non-EU buyers face in Italy versus Croatia?

EU and non-EU buyers face reciprocity tables in Italy identical nationwide with codice fiscale and notary rogito; Croatia allows EU direct purchase with legacy coastal restrictions for non-EU buyers often solved via local company structures with counsel review.









Italy requires MAECI reciprocity confirmation for US, UK, Canadian, Australian, and other listed nationals before rogito. Buyers obtain codice fiscale, open or designate euro wire paths, and execute notary-led rogito with 9% registration tax on second homes. Elective residence and investor visa routes exist but do not count passive property as qualifying investment.

Croatia assigns an OIB tax number, routes non-EU purchases through Ministry of Justice approval under bilateral reciprocity treaties, and completes transfer before land registry (zk.udruga / cadastre). US, UK, and Canadian buyers generally qualify under published reciprocity lists, but allow 30-60 days for approval on first purchase. Croatia offers temporary residence tied to property ownership for non-EU nationals in some cases, yet this is not a €250,000 Golden Visa equivalent to Greece.

UK buyers post-Brexit sit in the non-EU queue on both coasts. US buyers find familiar legal counsel channels in Milan, Rome, and Split but should not assume identical timelines. Australian and Canadian enquiry grew on Puglia stock specifically because Italian reciprocity is well documented and Ostuni agents publish English-language closing checklists.

## How do taxes and holding costs stack up for cross-border owners?

Headline transfer tax favours Croatia at 3% on resale property versus Italy's 9% registration tax on non-resident second homes. Annual holding costs reverse the story on many Italian rural purchases because IMU and IVAFE apply to cadastral values that lag market prices yet still exceed Croatian municipal property tax on equivalent Adriatic stock.

Italy charges IMU at 0.4-1.06% depending on cadastral category and primary-home status, plus IVAFE at 0.76% of Italian property value for non-residents without flat-tax election. Rental income often flows through cedolare secca at 21% (long-term) or 26% (short-term), simplifying filing for foreign landlords. Capital gains on sales within five years may trigger plusvalenza for Italian tax residents.

Croatia's 3% transfer tax on secondary resale stock (VAT applies on new builds from developers) keeps entry friction lower. Annual property tax remains modest relative to Western EU norms. Rental income taxation offers paušal (lump-sum) options for small landlords in some structures, but cross-border owners typically need Croatian tax advisers to avoid double reporting with home-country obligations.

| Charge | Italy (Puglia example) | Croatia (Split example) |
|--------|------------------------|-------------------------|

| Notary and fees | 1-2% + agency | 1-1.5% + agency |
| Annual property tax | IMU ~0.76-1.06% cadastral | Municipal tax (lower) |
| Non-resident wealth tax | IVAFE 0.76% | Not equivalent |
| Rental tax | Cedolare 21-26% | Progressive or lump-sum |
| Capital gains | Plusvalenza if resident | 20% or exemptions by hold |

Neither country eliminates home-country reporting. US citizens remain subject to FIRPTA-style considerations only on US assets, but worldwide income reporting continues. UK, Australian, and Canadian residents need treaty-aware modelling before choosing Adriatic jurisdiction for rental-heavy strategies.

## Which market offers better liquidity when you sell?

Liquidity determines whether paper gains survive contact with a buyer who needs mortgage approval and reciprocity clearance. Italy's depth in Milan, Rome, Florence, and established Puglia coastal towns exceeds Croatia's except in Split's fastest-moving segments.

Italy recorded sustained foreign buyer share in southern regions, with Puglia leading international transaction counts. Ostuni and Carovigno maintain active Anglo-German-American broker networks, Brindisi and Bari airports support inspection trips, and FIAIP data gives agents comparable sold-price references. Average marketing periods for well-priced Puglia stock run 6-12 months; Milan prime can move faster.

Croatia's euro adoption in 2023 reduced currency friction and improved mortgage standardisation for EU buyers. Split and Zadar see faster turnover than Dubrovnik old-town stock, where trophy buyers are fewer and seasonality concentrates demand. Island properties (Hvar, Korčula) can sit 9-18 months without price cuts unless marketed aggressively to diaspora buyers. Istria benefits from Slovenian and Austrian drive-to demand with better liquidity than southern Dalmatian islands.

Investors with 7-10 year holds who may exit to another international buyer should weight Italy's Puglia liquidity premium. Investors buying Croatian property primarily for personal use with optional rent cover accept longer exit timelines in exchange for lower transfer tax at entry.

## Dubrovnik vs Puglia: which Adriatic coast fits your brief?

Dubrovnik sells global brand recognition, dramatic walled-city tourism, and cruise-ship volume that supports premium nightly rates in July and August. Puglia sells longer seasons, airport connectivity, yield-friendly entry tickets, and Italy's broader resale universe.

Dubrovnik investors typically accept 3-4.5% gross yields on €400,000-600,000 apartments because personal use and prestige dominate the spreadsheet. STR caps and neighbor pressure in the old town require careful unit selection; periphery and Župa dubrovačka offer more square metres per euro with ferry-noise trade-offs.

Puglia investors target 5-7% gross on masserie, trulli conversions, and coastal villas with pools, leveraging April-October demand and food-tourism tailwinds. Ostuni, Carovigno, and Cisternino provide established foreign-buyer infrastructure documented in our [Puglia property investment guide](/guides/puglia-property-investment-guide/). Renovation risk (trulli permits, olive-tree protection) replaces Dubrovnik's overtourism regulatory risk.

Choose Dubrovnik if you prioritise UNESCO glamour, short personal-use windows, and can hold through low net yields. Choose Puglia if you prioritise income, renovation upside, and exit optionality within Italy's national buyer pool.
