## Rental Yields and Total Returns

Gross yield on portal listings excludes IMU, cedolare secca at 21% or 26%, condominium spese, and realistic vacancy — model net cash flow with commercialista before comparing tickets across communes. Furnished twelve-month contracts and licensed STR paths carry different tax bands and compliance filings that can shift net returns by 150-200 basis points on identical purchase prices.

### National yield picture

Italy's national gross rental yield average sits near 4.3% according to aggregated portal data cited in our [Italy rental yield guide](/guides/italy-rental-yield-guide/). France's national average compresses toward 3.5%, pulled down by Paris and Riviera pricing. On a €500,000 apartment, the 0.8 percentage-point spread equals €4,000 additional gross rent per year before tax and management.

Italy's yield advantage is regional, not automatic in trophy addresses. Milan long-term gross yields run 3-5%; Rome prime runs 2.5-5% depending on STR compliance. France's Paris long-term yields often sit at 2.5-3.5% in central arrondissements, while Nice and Cannes coastal stock rarely exceeds 3.5% gross on well-bought units.

Total return investors should add capital appreciation. Italy recorded roughly 2.6% annual price growth nationally versus France at 2.1% over 2022-2025. Combined total returns near 6.9% in Italy versus 5.6% in France on national averages, before tax and currency effects (both euro-zone).

Paris and Milan are the default comparison for investors who want a major city with international schools, corporate tenants, and resale recognition. They are not interchangeable.

Milan is Italy's finance and fashion capital. City asking prices averaged €5,653/m² in April 2026, with Brera, Porta Nuova, and Navigli trading €4,500-7,500/m². Foreign buyers represent roughly 22% of central transactions. Long-term gross yields of 3-5% sit on year-round corporate and university demand. See our [Milan property investment guide](/guides/milan-property-investment-guide/) for district breakdowns.

Paris remains Europe's most expensive major residential market on a per-metre basis. Prime arrondissements (6th, 7th, 16th) commonly list €10,000-14,000/m², with trophy boulevard-facing stock exceeding €18,000/m². Gross yields of 2.5-3.5% reflect decades of global safe-haven demand. Liquidity is deep but marketing periods stretch when sellers overshoot comparables.

## Tax Architecture: IFI France vs IMU Italy

Tax treatment separates look-alike lifestyle purchases into different net-return outcomes.

### France: IFI and holding costs

France's Impôt sur la Fortune Immobilière (IFI) applies to net real-estate wealth above €1.3 million at sliding rates from 0.5% to 1.5%. A portfolio with €2.5M in French property faces meaningful annual IFI even if rental income is modest. Local taxe foncière and income tax on rent at progressive rates up to 45% (plus social charges) further compress net yields.

Non-resident landlords face withholding on French-source rental income. Structuring through SCI companies is common but adds accounting cost and does not eliminate IFI on personal wealth thresholds.

### Italy: IMU, IVAFE, and rental tax

Italy charges IMU (municipal property tax) at roughly 0.4-1.06% of cadastral value depending on category and comune. Non-residents pay IVAFE at 0.76% on the value of Italian property holdings. Rental income can be taxed at flat 21% cedolare secca on residential leases or progressive IRPEF rates.

For a €1.5M Italian portfolio, annual IMU plus IVAFE typically falls below equivalent French IFI exposure on the same nominal value. Above €3M net real-estate wealth, French IFI can exceed Italian combined charges, making Italy relatively attractive for sub-ultra-HNW portfolios.

| Tax item | France | Italy |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Wealth tax trigger | €1.3M net RE | No net-wealth tax on RE |
| Annual property charge | Taxe foncière + IFI | IMU 0.4-1.06% |
| Non-resident surcharge | Social charges on rent | IVAFE 0.76% on property value |
| Rental income option | Progressive up to 45% | Cedolare secca 21% flat |
| Purchase tax (second home) | 5-6% droits de mutation | 9% registration tax |

Always model with a cross-border tax adviser. Treaty relief, domicile elections, and company structures change outcomes materially.

## Residency and Golden Visa Context

Neither Italy nor France offers a Greece-style Golden Visa where passive real-estate purchase alone grants residency.

Greece's program at €250,000 in qualifying areas (recently raised in prime zones) remains the Southern European benchmark for property-linked residency. Italy's investor visa requires €250,000 in an innovative startup or €500,000 in an established company, not passive bricks and mortar. France offers talent and business visas but no pure property route.

For non-EU buyers seeking EU access through real estate alone, Greece beats both Italy and France. For buyers who already hold EU access or treat property as a financial asset, Italy and France compete on yield, lifestyle, and tax rather than visa mechanics.

## Foreign Buyer Rules and Purchase Process

Italy and France both welcome foreign capital with mature notary systems.

EU citizens purchase on identical terms to nationals in both jurisdictions. Non-EU buyers face no general ban in France. Italy requires reciprocity agreements, which cover the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and most developed nations. Both countries allow company ownership through local or EU entities.

Transaction timelines run 3-6 months in France versus 4-9 months in Italy depending on urbanistica checks and mortgage approval. Total costs land at 10-13% in France and 9-11% in Italy for second-home buyers.

Due diligence priorities differ slightly. France emphasises diagnostics (amiante, plomb, termites) and copropriété charges. Italy emphasises conformità catastale, abusi edilizi clearance, and cadastral category verification. ## Market Liquidity and Exit Strategies

France's Paris and Riviera markets absorb international marketing but punish overpricing. Expect 6-12 months on well-priced central Paris apartments and 4-8 months on correctly valued Nice sea-view units.

Italy's Milan centro resells in 4-8 months on clean title with foreign buyer share near 22%. Rome benefits from Jubilee 2026 enquiry surges. Southern regions trade slower at 9-18 months.

For investors planning a 7-10 year hold with exit optionality, Milan and Paris both work. For 15-year legacy holds, Riviera and Liguria trophy stock rewards patience over speed.

## Pros and Cons Summary

### Italy property investment pros and cons

**Pros:** Higher national gross yields (about 4.3% vs 3.5% in France), lower entry in Milan versus Paris, IMU and IVAFE often lighter than French IFI on mid-tier portfolios, strong renovation upside in Puglia and Sicily, and deep foreign buyer liquidity in Milan and Rome.

**Cons:** Longer due diligence on conformità catastale and abusi edilizi, slower bureaucracy on urbanistica permits, and STR regulation tightening in UNESCO cities.

### France property investment pros and cons

**Pros:** Paris and Côte d'Azur global brand prestige, mature foreign-buyer services, faster transactions in some coastal markets, and established copropriété legal frameworks.

**Cons:** Lower gross yields in prime segments (2.5-3.5%), higher absolute prices in Paris and Nice, IFI wealth tax on property above €1.3M, and Riviera STR caps in dense tourist zones.

## Decision Framework: Choose Italy or France

### Choose Italy if:

- You prioritise gross yield (4.3% national average vs 3.5% in France).
- Your budget fits Milan or Liguria better than Paris or the Côte d'Azur.
- You accept Italian bureaucratic depth in exchange for renovation upside and IMU/IVAFE rather than IFI.
- You want exposure to emerging southern regions (Puglia, Sicily) alongside a gateway city unit.

### Choose France if:

- You need Paris or Riviera address prestige for personal or family use.
- You have existing French tax residency and advisory relationships.
- You accept 2.5-3.5% yields in prime segments as the price of global brand recognition.
- IFI exposure is manageable within your wider wealth structure.

### Portfolio approach

Sophisticated Mediterranean allocators often combine a Milan or Rome income unit (Italy) with a Provence or Atlantic coast lifestyle base (France), hedging regulatory tightening in both STR markets while keeping euro-zone real asset exposure diversified.
