## Golden Visa: Greece's Decisive Advantage

Greece's Golden Visa program grants five-year renewable residency to non-EU investors who purchase qualifying real estate from €250,000 in most regions (thresholds rose in Athens, Thessaloniki, Mykonos, and Santorini after August 2024). Spouses and dependent children qualify. The property can be rented. Schengen travel access follows.

Italy offers no parallel route for passive real estate. The Italian investor visa requires €250,000 in an innovative startup or €500,000 in an established Italian company with job-creation conditions. Elective residence visa paths exist for passive-income retirees but do not treat a property purchase alone as qualifying investment.

For non-EU families where EU residency is the primary objective, Greece wins decisively. For buyers who already hold EU passports or treat residency as secondary, Italy competes on asset quality and exit depth rather than visa mechanics. ## Rental Yields: Athens vs Italian Cities

Greece's yield advantage concentrates in Athens value districts and secondary mainland towns, not on trophy islands.

Athens neighbourhoods including Koukaki, Mets, and emerging Kypseli corridors deliver 4.5-6% gross on apartments bought at €2,200-3,800/m². Long-term expat and digital-nomad demand supports year-round tenancy outside pure tourism plays.

Italy's national average sits at 4.3% per our [Italy rental yield guide](/guides/italy-rental-yield-guide/). Milan runs 3-5%; Rome 2.5-5%; Puglia and Sicily exceed 5% on coastal and historic-town stock. Italy's yield spread is wider: gateway cities compress while the south compensates.

### Yield comparison by segment

| Market segment | Italy gross yield | Greece gross yield | Notes |
|----------------|-------------------|--------------------|-------|
| Gateway city prime | Milan 3-5%, Rome 2.5-5% | Athens central 4-5.5% | Athens wins on yield |
| Island trophy | Capri, Costa Smeralda 2.5-4% | Mykonos, Santorini 3-4% | Both low yield, high prestige |
| Mainland value coast | Puglia 5-6%, Sicily 4.5-5.5% | Crete 4-5%, Peloponnese 4-5% | Italy slightly higher |
| Historic UNESCO town | Florence 3-4%, Siena 4-5% | Rhodes old town 4-5% | Comparable STR risk |

Net yields after tax, ENFIA or IMU, management, and platform fees typically sit 1.5-2.5 points below gross in both countries.

## Greek Islands vs Italian Coastal Regions

Island comparisons dominate buyer conversations because both countries sell blue-water lifestyle at scale.

### Greek islands: Mykonos, Santorini, Crete

Mykonos and Santorini command €4,500-8,000/m² on sea-view stock with gross yields of 3-4% despite premium nightly rates. Seasonality is extreme: 70-80% of STR income may concentrate June-September. New Golden Visa thresholds in these islands pushed entry above €800,000 in prime zones, reducing pure visa arbitrage.

Crete offers larger land mass, year-round resident population, and €1,800-3,200/m² value stock with 4-5% yields. Heraklion and Chania attract expat retirees and remote workers with milder seasonality than Cyclades specks.

### Italian counterparts: Sardinia, Sicily, Puglia

Sardinia's Costa Smeralda competes directly with Mykonos on glamour pricing (€5,000-12,000/m²) with similar yield compression. Sicily delivers 4.5-5.5% yields on €900-1,800/m² stock in Syracuse, Taormina hinterland, and Palermo regeneration zones. Puglia's Valle d'Itria and Ostuni corridors reach 5-6% with growing British and German buyer depth.

Italy's advantage is infrastructure: more airports, motorways, high-speed rail links to Milan and Rome, and EU Recovery Fund spending on southern ports. Ferry dependency is lower except on smaller Italian islands. ## Liquidity and Exit Strategies

Liquidity separates look-alike yield spreadsheets from realisable gains.

Italy's Milan market absorbed roughly 89,000 Lombardy residential transactions in 2025 with foreign buyers near 22% of central Milan deals. Rome saw Jubilee-linked enquiry growth of 44.7% in 2026. Florence, Tuscany, and Puglia coastal towns maintain established Anglo-German resale channels.

Greece's Athens market deepened after 2020 with foreign buyer share rising, yet average marketing periods exceed Milan for equivalent price points. Island trophy properties can linger 12-24 months unless priced aggressively. Golden Visa buyers sometimes overpay at entry, compressing exit margins.

For investors with 7-10 year hold horizons who may sell to another international buyer, Italy's gateway cities and mainstream coasts win. For 15-year Golden Visa holders treating the apartment as a residency anchor with rental side income, Greek liquidity constraints matter less.

## Tax and Holding Costs

Italy applies IMU at 0.4-1.06% of cadastral value depending on category and comune, plus IVAFE at 0.76% for non-residents on Italian property value. Residential rental income can use 21% cedolare secca.

Greece applies ENFIA annual property tax based on assessed value, generally modest on mid-tier apartments. Rental income faces progressive taxation up to 44% for Greek tax residents; non-resident landlords withhold at source.

Golden Visa holders who become Greek tax residents trigger worldwide income reporting. Italy's flat-tax regime for new residents (€100,000 annual substitute tax on foreign income) appeals to a different HNW segment than Golden Visa buyers, but both require cross-border tax planning.

| Charge | Italy | Greece |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Annual property tax | IMU 0.4-1.06% | ENFIA (value-based) |
| Non-resident surcharge | IVAFE 0.76% | Withholding on rent |
| Rental income flat option | Cedolare 21% | No equivalent |
| Purchase tax (second home) | 9% registration | 3.09% transfer + VAT on new builds |

## Foreign Buyer Process and Due Diligence

Greece assigns an AFM tax number, uses a notary transfer, and registers at the local cadastre. Building permits on islands require extra scrutiny for illegal construction legacy from pre-2011 stock.

Italy requires codice fiscale, notary rogito, and conformità verification against abusi edilizi risk. Non-EU buyers need reciprocity clearance. Both countries budget 9-12% closing costs.

Greek due diligence emphasises clear title in a still-maturing cadastre system on some islands. Italian due diligence emphasises urbanistica sanatoria and heritage restrictions in centro storico zones. Neither market forgives skipped legal review.

## Risk Perception and Sovereign Context

International buyers still price sovereign and banking risk differently despite shared euro membership.

Greece's 2015 crisis memory lingers in older investor cohorts, though Athens prices recovered and foreign enquiry normalized. Island oversupply in new-build Golden Visa blocks creates localized price pressure.

Italy carries political volatility headlines but Milan and Rome trade as core euro-zone real assets with institutional bid support. Southern Italy adds earthquake and landslide due diligence in Apennine and coastal zones, comparable to Greek seismic codes on islands.

Conservative allocators favour Italy for brand-name exits. Value hunters accept Greek cyclicality for Golden Visa plus yield bundle.

## Decision Framework

### Choose Greece if:

- EU residency through property is your primary goal (Golden Visa).
- You target Athens value yields at 4.5-6% with moderate liquidity acceptance.
- You want island lifestyle with full Schengen access on a €250,000-500,000 budget.
- You plan long holds where visa benefit outweighs resale speed.

### Choose Italy if:

- Investment returns and Milan-Rome-Florence liquidity matter more than residency.
- You want southern yields (Puglia, Sicily 5-6%) without island ferry dependency.
- You need heritage brand recognition (Tuscany, Rome, Lake Como) for future exits.
- You already hold EU citizenship and do not need a property-linked visa.
